More on Bonds

In our previous class we have seen that combining energy level $n=2$ atomic states, results in sigma bonds and anti-bonds $\sigma2p_x$, $\sigma^*2p_x$, which charge density is largest along the molecular axis, as well as pi bonds $\sigma^*2p_y$, $\pi^*2p_z$, whose charge molecular density is off axis:



$\sigma$ bonds are stronger bonds due to head-on overlapping of orbitals on two different atoms. $\pi$ bonds are weaker and are due to lateral overlap between p (or d) orbitals.

Second, energy value (or level) for $\sigma$ and $\pi$ bonds differ from molecule to molecule. For instance, $\sigma 2 p$ is higher on $N_2$ than on $O_2$ and $\pi 2 p$ is the other way around. Third, electrons at $\pi^8 2p$ states in $O_2$ occupy different states, because electrons prefer to spread out. Lastly, as we saw in the $n=1$ case, the total bonding power comes from having mostly bound states that anti-bound states, or mathematically $$n_{\rm bond}-n_{\rm anti-bond} > 0\,.$$

(Some) Types of Bounds

  • A Covalent bond consists of the mutual sharing of pairs of electrons between two atoms. These electrons are simultaneously attracted by the two atomic nuclei

  • Polar covalent bonds occur between atoms of different elements. For instance, when HF forms, hydrogen's $n=1$ and fluorine $n=2$ electrons are shared asymmetrically. The fluorine has an excess of electrons, leaving an equal positive excess at the hydrogen end.
  • Ionic bond occurs when a (usually large) atom with a lone valence electron meet an atom one electron short of noble gas structure, in such a way that the almost noble gas steal the lane valence electron of the large atom



  • Hybridization is the idea that atomic orbitals fuse to form newly hybridized orbitals.

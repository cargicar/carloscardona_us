Indistinguishability

What is the wave function of a two-particle system?. We need to solve the Schrodinger equation for it. $$-{\hbar\over 2m}\left({\partial^2\over\partial x_1^2}+{\partial^2\over\partial x_1^2}\right)\Psi(x_1,x_2)+U(x_1,x_2)\Psi(x_1,x_2)= E\Psi(x_1,x_2) $$ Lets apply this to a infinite well. By doing the very crude approximation $U(x_1,x_2)\sim U(x_1)+U(x_2)$, we get: $$\psi_{n_i}=\sqrt{2\over L}{\rm sin}{n_i\pi\,x_i\over L} $$ with $i=1,2$. Which corresponds to the solution $$\Psi(x_1, x_2)=\psi(x_1)_{n_1}\psi(x_2)_{n_2} $$ and $$E={n_1^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2 m L}+{n_2^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2 m L} $$

What about probability?

Fundamental particles are indistinguishable. Every electron in the universe is identical to every other electron.
Say we have two electrons in a box, label 1 and 2. Then we measure that one of them is at level $n=3$. Which is it at that level, 1 or 2?.
since we can't distinguish them, we don't know, i.e, we can't compute the probability as a sum of two independent probabilities $P_1+P_2$. We should be free to swap particle 1 and 2 without changing the total probability.
We then need to define a symmetric probability, that does not change when we swap 1 and 2. It turns out, there are two ways of doing that:

$$ \boxed{\Psi_S(x_1, x_2)=\psi_{n_1}(x_1)\psi_{n_2}(x_2)+\psi_{n_1}(x_2)\psi_{n_2}(x_1)\,\qquad{\rm Symmetric}} $$ $$ \boxed{\Psi_A(x_1, x_2)=\psi_{n_1}(x_1)\psi_{n_2}(x_2)-\psi_{n_1}(x_2)\psi_{n_2}(x_1)\,\qquad{\rm Anti-symmetric} }$$ which produce probabilites: $$P_S(x_1,x_2)=\psi_S^2(x_1,x_2)=(\psi_{n_1}(x_1)\psi_{n_2}(x_2))^2+(\psi_{n_1}(x_2)\psi_{n_2}(x_1))^2 $$ $$+2\psi_{n_1}(x_1)\psi_{n_2}(x_2)\psi_{n_1}(x_2)\psi_{n_2}(x_1) $$ and $$P_A(x_1,x_2)=\psi_A^2(x_1,x_2)=(\psi_{n_1}(x_1)\psi_{n_2}(x_2))^2+(\psi_{n_1}(x_2)\psi_{n_2}(x_1))^2 $$ $$-2\psi_{n_1}(x_1)\psi_{n_2}(x_2)\psi_{n_1}(x_2)\psi_{n_2}(x_1) $$

Spin-Statistics Theorem

The theorem states that:
  • The wave function of a system of identical integer-spin particles has the same value when the positions of any two particles are swapped. Particles with wave functions symmetric under exchange are called bosons.
  • The wave function of a system of identical half-integerâ€“spin particles changes sign when two particles are swapped. Particles with wave functions antisymmetric under exchange are called fermions.

Pauli Exclusion Principle

Consider a system of two fermions occupying states $n_1$ and $n_2$. If $n_1=n_2$: $$\Psi_A(x_1, x_2)=\psi_{n_1}(x_1)\psi_{n_1}(x_2)-\psi_{n_1}(x_2)\psi_{n_1}(x_1)=0. $$ Exclusion principle: No two indistinguishable fermions may occupy the same individual-particle state.
- Wolfgang Pauli (Nobel 1945).

Composite particles

We can combine fundamental particles in order to form composite particles. which satisfies the following ``logic'' table, for positive intenger $n$:

$(n)-$Bosons ↣ Composite Boson
$(2n)-$Fermions ↣ Composite Boson
$(2n+1)-$Fermions ↣ Composite Fermion

Examples include the proton (1/2), the neutron (1/2), Bose-Einstein (1). Several composite particles, have powerful (potential) technological applications.

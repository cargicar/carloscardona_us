Experimental Evidence of Angular Momentum Quantization

Both, Bohr's model and the full quantum treatment of the hydrogen atom, led us to unavoidably consider the idea of angular momentum quatization. So now we will need to look for an experiment that confirm this claim. Before moving into that, we need to remember few basic facts about rotating charge particles.

Angular momentum and magnetic dipole

Lets again imagine the electron orbitin a proton. Since it is a charge particle, it will produce a magnetic dipole moment $\mu= I A$.

Which can be used to write $$\vec{\mu}=-{e\over 2 m_e}\vec{ L}.$$ The minus sign is a convention. With this, we can measure the force felt by a charged particle under the influence of a magnetic field: $\vec{F}=-\vec{\nabla} U$, with, $$ U=-\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B}\,,$$ then by using the relation between magnetic dipole moment and angular momenta, we should see a quantization of force by passing atoms through a magnetic field,

The Stern-Gerlach Experiment

Assuming the magnetic field is oriented in the $z$ direction, we have, $$\vec{F}=-{e\over 2 m_e}m_{\ell}\,\hbar\, \partial_z B_z \hat{z},\qquad-\ell\leq m_{\ell}\leq \ell $$
Stern-Gerlach experiment.

The experiment confirm sucesfully the quantization of angular momenta in atoms, seeing discrete splitings along the z-axis of the screen for $\ell=1,2,\cdots$, but it found an incredible surprise.

Spin

When $\ell=0$ we know that $m_{\ell}=0$, and we should see a single small cloud right in the middle of the screen. But the Stern-Gerlach experiment produces something like the second box below:

It was an indication of the intrinsic magnetic dipole moment of the electron! or the Spin, which satsifies the following,
  • Similarly as orbital angular momenta, spin is quantized in terms of a discrete, but not neccesarilly integer $s$ by can be semi-integer, $$|S|=\hbar\sqrt{s(s+1)}.$$ Also, similarly to $L_z$, we have $$S_z=\hbar m_s\,\quad m_{s}= -s,-s+1,\cdots,s $$
  • Unlike $\ell$, $s$ is not a quantum number that can be changed by say, excite an state, but is number that depends on the fundamental particle we are considering. An electron for example has $s=1/2$, whereas a photon has $s=1$.
  • The intrinsic magnetic dipole $\mu_S=g{q\over 2m}S$, where $g$ is known as the gyromagnetic radius $$
Returning to the Stern-Gerlach experiment, what they really observed at $\ell=0$ was, $$\vec{F}=-{e\over m_e}m_{s}\,\hbar\, \partial_z B_z \hat{z},\qquad-s \leq m_{\ell}\leq s, $$ since for the electron $s=1/2$, then $m_s=\pm {1\over 2}$

Spin ad Wave funtions

All in all, we have just found that in our description of the wave function for an electron in an hyrogen atom, we are missing one quantum number, i.e, each state should be labeled by the quantum numbers $(n,\ell,m_{\ell},m_{s})$. Fortunately, for the case of the electron, only two values of $m_s$ are allowed, and we can just split our wave functions as, $$ \psi_{n,\ell,m_{\ell}}^{+}\qquad {\rm spin\,\,up}\,\qquad \psi_{n,\ell,m_{\ell}}^{-}\qquad {\rm spin\,\,down} $$

Classical vs Quantum

Up until this point we have seen that the probability distribution of energy is described by the Boltzmann distribution, which is in a sense a semi-classical expression. We have used it to compute averages of some quantities both, semi-classically and classically. In this section we are going to see a proper quantum energy probability distribution. It is enlightening to make a quick comparison between the different assumptions which should make in a full quantity system versus a classical system,
  • Quantum: Discrete Energies
    Classical: Continuous Energies
  • Quantum: Energy degeneracies
    Classical: Not degeneracies
  • Quantum: Indistinguishable particles
    Classical: Distiguishable particles


Once again, we want compute the average number of particles at energy level $E_i$ (occupation number). For that, think we have a system of total energy $E$, total number of particles $N$ and put $n_i$ particles at a given energy $E_i$. Henceforth we need to distribute the remaining $E-n_i$ among the remaining $N-n_i$. Say there is $W(E-n_i,N-n_i)$ different ways of achieving such configuration, then: $$P(E_i)={W(E-n_i,N-n_i)\over \sum_i W(E-n_i,N-n_i)}\,. $$ An important observation is that $n_i << N $, and we can compute $P(E_i)$ approximately, by expanding in small $n_i$. More explicitly, defining, $$f(E(n_i),N(n_i))=\log W(E-n_i,N-n_i)\,, $$ and Taylor expanding at first order in $n_i$ we get, $$f(n_i<< N)=\log W(E,N)+n_i\left((-E_i){\partial\over \partial E}-1{\partial\over \partial N} \right)\log W(E,N)$$ Finally, using $S=k_B \log W$ and the definitions, $$ {\partial S\over \partial Q}={1\over k_B T},\qquad ={1\over k_B} {\partial S\over \partial N}=\alpha\,, $$ where $\alpha$ is the chemical potential, we arrive to $$W(E-n_i,N-n_i)=W(E,N)e^{-(\alpha+E_i/k_B T)n_i}\,. $$ Now, in order to compute the occupation number, $$\bar{n_i}=\mathcal{N}(E_i)=\sum_in_i P(E_i)\,, $$ we need to carry over the sum. This can be done easily, as we have seen in previous classes, by using the geometric series, leading us to an unifiying expression for the three energy distributions we have learn so far: $$ \mathcal{N}(E_i)={1\over B e^{E_i/k_B T}-\epsilon}$$ with $\epsilon=1$ for boson, $\epsilon=-1$ for fermions, and $\epsilon=0$ for classical/semiclassical particles!. $B$ is a normalization constant that can be computed from the requierement of total probability equal to one: $$B_{bosons}={1\over 1-e} $$

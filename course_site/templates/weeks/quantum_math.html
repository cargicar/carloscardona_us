Some quick reminder: Statistical central values

Say we are interested in a quantity $Q$, which has three possible values $Q_1,\,Q_2,\,Q_3$, each with probability of occur $P_1,\,P_2,\,P_3$, in other words: if we perform a large $N$ of trys, we will have \begin{equation} P_i={N_i/N} \end{equation} Obviously, $N_i< N$ and therefore \begin{equation} \sum_i P_i \leq 1 \end{equation} When all possible values of $Q$ are considered, then the sum above becomes one.

The mean value of a (random) variable, is defined as, \begin{equation} \bar{Q}=\sum_iQ_i P_i \end{equation} and similarly, the mean value of a function of the given variable is given by \begin{equation} \overline{f(Q)}=\sum_if(Q_i)P_i\,. \end{equation} The standard deviation quantify how much the values taken by a (random) variable deviates from the mean $Q_i-\bar{Q}$, however, since some deviations are positive and other negatives, it does not make sense to average over the distance, but its more meaninful to average over the square distances: \begin{equation} \sigma_Q=\sqrt{\sum(Q_i-\bar{Q})^2P_i} \end{equation} If the random variable being considered takes continuos values, the sums become integrals.

Expectation Values, Uncertainties and Operators in QM

The expectation value of a variable $x$ is defined as that quantum mean value of such quantity: \begin{equation} \bar{x}=\int_{\rm all~~space}xP(x)^2dx=\int_{\rm all~~space}x|\psi(x)|^2dx \end{equation} The uncertainty of a variable $x$ is given by the quantum standard deviation: \begin{equation} \sigma_x=\sqrt{\int_{\rm all~~space}(x-\bar{x})^2|\psi(x)|^2dx} \end{equation} With some algebra, the uncertainty can be written in the following more convenient form: \begin{equation} \sigma_x=\sqrt{\overline{x^2}-\bar{x}^2} \end{equation} The expectation value of a function of a variable $x$, is given by: \begin{equation} \overline{f(x)}=\int_{\rm all~~space}f(x)|\psi(x)|^2dx\,. \end{equation} More generally, in QM we define observable as the quantities that can be measure. Mathematically, those observables are represented by operators action on the wave function of the particle, such as momentum, which is defined by the following differential operator: \begin{equation} \hat{p}=-i\hbar {\partial\over\partial_x}\,. \end{equation} Operators are usually denoted by a hat. Once an operator is determined in its mathematical form, we can compute its associated statistial central values by the obvious generalizations, such us, expectation value of operator $\hat{Q}$ is given by: \begin{equation} \overline{Q}=\int_{\rm all~~space}\Psi^*(x,t)\hat{ Q}\Psi(x,t) dx \end{equation} and its uncertanty by \begin{equation} \sigma_Q=\sqrt{\overline{Q}^2-\bar{Q}^2}\,. \end{equation}

Particle-Wave duality: A closer look

So far we have considered either the wave or particle nature of an object separatly, but a more realistic picture, which represent both at once, is given by a wave package, or pulse, which corresponds to a linear combination of plane waves.


The most simple example: \begin{equation} \Psi(x,t)=A e^{i(k_1 x-\omega_1 t)}+A e^{i(k_2 x-\omega_2 t)} \end{equation} such us there is only a small deviation between them two, i.e $k_1=k_0+dk$ and $k_2=k_0-dk$, similarly $\omega_1=\omega_0+d\omega$ and $\omega_2=\omega_0-d\omega$. Replacing this values back, and simplifying a bit, we arrive to, \begin{equation} \Psi(x,t)=2Ae^{i(k_0x-\omega_0 t)}~{\rm cos}[x dk-td\omega]\,. \end{equation}


Particlelike waves: Fourier expansion

In general, any wave function can be written in terms of a Fourier expansion (or transform) as, \begin{equation} \Psi(x,t)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}A(k)e^{i(kx-\omega t)}dk \end{equation} Let us recall what $\omega$ is: The Schroedinger equation for a free particle is given by: \begin{equation} -{\hbar^2\over 2m}{\partial^2\Psi(x,t)\over \partial x}=i\hbar{\partial\Psi(x,t)\over\partial t}\,. \end{equation} Applying it on a single plane wave component, we get ${\hbar^2 k^2\over 2m}=\hbar \omega$, or equivalently \begin{equation} \omega(k)={\hbar k^2\over 2 m} \end{equation} The relantionship between frecuency and wave number is know as a dispersion relation. For comparison purposes, let us recall that for a massless particle $E=cp$, therefore \begin{equation} \omega_{\rm photon}(k)=ck \end{equation} We still have an answer question what is the speed of a wave?. On the one hand, we can computed by dimensional analysis $[l]/[t]\quad\rightarrow\quad \lambda f$, which is defined as the phase velocity: \begin{equation} v_{\rm phase}={\omega\over k}\,. \end{equation} Then we have, for the photon $v_{\rm phase}=c$ but for a massive particle $v_{\rm phase}={\hbar k\over 2 m}$. Whereas EM waves move at $v_{\rm phase}=c$ matter waves can move faster than light?? 😨. Yes, they might, but remember, the wave function is a convenient description, and the actual physical quantities are the probabilities. Take for example the simpler linear combination of two planes waves having wave number centered around $k_0$. \begin{equation} |\Psi(x,t)|^2=4A^2~{\rm cos}^2[x dk-td\omega]\,. \end{equation} We can see that in $|\Psi(x,t)|^2$ both $\omega_0$ and $k_0$ are cancelled, so there is not phase velocity associated to such probability, however, there is still a velocity we can associated to it ${d\omega\over dk}$, which is known as the group velocity. Hencefort, in QM mechanics we are not concerned with the phase velocity but with the group velocity \begin{equation} v_{g}={d\omega(k)\over dk}(k_0) \end{equation} where $k_0$ is the one related to the speed of the particle, in other words, if the speed of the particle is $v_0=p_0/m=\hbar k_0/m$ (which is obviously less that $c$ 😅 ). Also notice that the group velocity for a photon \begin{equation} v_{g}={d\omega(k)\over dk}(k_0)={d(c k)\over dk}(k_0)=c \end{equation} and is still equal to $c$.

No matter what is the phase velocity of the individual plane waves composing a wave function, the probability will move at the speed associated to the particle 🤯

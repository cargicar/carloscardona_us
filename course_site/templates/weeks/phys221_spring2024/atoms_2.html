Hydrogen Spectral Lines

In this session we will start our Quantum Mechanical treatment for the Hydrogen atom. We can start by thinking about it as light negative charged particle (electron) orbiting a heavy (2000 heavier proton) positively charged particle. With those assumptions, we can think of the electron as moving around the proton under the influence of the central potential \begin{equation} u(r)=-{1\over4\pi \epsilon_0}{e^2\over r}\, \end{equation} which looks like


which pretty much looks like a deep well. But with a non-constant potential energy. We can expect some general features from the well to happen here, like energy quantization and stationary solutions, but in order to ge the actual quantum states, we need to solve the Schrodinger equation for the given potential rigorously. Before doing that, lets look a bit deeper into the experimental observations on the hydrogen atom, previous to its quantum mechanical description.

Balmer Spectral Series

When looking into the spectra decomposition of light emitted by a hydrogen lamp


the following spectrum was observed:


which corresponds to wavelenghts 410nm, 434nm, 486nm, 656nm. By fitting those number into a formula, Johann Balmer found that those wavelenghts obey the relation, \begin{equation} {1\over \lambda}=R\left({1\over 4}-{1\over n^2}\right)\,\qquad n=3,4,5,\cdots. \end{equation} with $R=1.097\times 10^7m^{-1}=$Rydberg constant, which was adjusted so the formula fit the right observed values.

Previous to the 1900's, we didn't have a satisfactory model of the atom, and things got worse when the Rutherford experiment in 1909 discovered the atomic nucleus, ruling out the accepted plum pudding model of the time,



We have seen previously that's Bohr's model sucesfully explain Balmer's formula, let's see how: \begin{equation} \boxed{ E_{Bohr}=-{m e^2\over2 (4\pi\epsilon_0)^2 n^2}={-13.6 eV\over n^2} } \end{equation} -13.6 eV =ionization energy of the hydrogen atom. (BB)



We have found the stationary hydrogen's wavefunction solutions, however, in practice, an atom is never absolutely isolated, and interactions with the environment leads to energy transitions between levels, even quantum fluctuations lead to such transitions.

Each element in nature have a characteristic spectral emmision decomposition, which is used as a ``fingerprint'' that allow us to determine the chemical content of many substances. This is for example, how we deduce the elemtary content of starts, galaxies, cosmic dust, etc.

Despite Bohr's model provides with a good explanation for the quantization of energy, it still left some things unexplained, like quantization of angular momentum. It's also very limited, since it does not generalize to other atoms and a more rigurous quantum treatment of the hydrogen atom is requiered. Let us recall that the potential energy for the hydrogen atom can be taken to be the electrostatic potential felt by the electron, \begin{equation} U(r)=-{1\over 4\pi\epsilon_0}{e^2\over r}\,. \end{equation} We see that it only depends on the radius (a potential that only depends on the radius, is called a central potential), and henceforth, it would be unconvenient to use cartesian coordinates, and we need to rewrite Schrodinger equation in the more convenient Spherical coordinates,



For the Schrodinger equation we need the operator, \begin{equation} \nabla^2={1\over r^2}\left[{\partial\over \partial r}\left(r^2{\partial\over \partial r}\right)+{1\over {\rm sin}(\theta)}{\partial\over \partial\theta}\left({\rm sin}(\theta){\partial\over\partial\theta}\right) +{1\over {\rm sin}^2(\theta)}{\partial^2\over \partial\phi^2}\right] \end{equation} In order to compute probabilities, we will also need the Integration measure, \begin{equation} dV=r^2 \,{\rm sin\theta}\,d\theta\,\phi\, dr \end{equation} 3D Schrodinger equation takes the form, \begin{equation} -{\hbar^2\over 2 m}{1\over r^2}\left[{\partial\over \partial r}\left(r^2{\partial\over \partial r}\right)+{1\over {\rm sin}(\theta)}{\partial\over \partial\theta}\left({\rm sin}(\theta){\partial\over\partial\theta}\right) +{1\over {\rm sin}^2(\theta)}{\partial^2\over \partial\phi^2}\right]\psi(r,\theta,\phi)+U(r)\psi(r,\theta,\phi)=E\psi(r,\theta,\phi) \end{equation} In order to solve this large equation, we will use our old separation of variables method, \begin{equation} \psi(r,\theta,\phi)=R(r)\Theta(\theta)\Phi(\phi)\,, \end{equation} which lead us to the following independent differential equations, $$-{\partial\over \partial r}\left(r^2{\partial\over \partial r}\right)R(r)-{2mr^2\over \hbar^2}(E-U(r))R(r)=C\,R(r)$$
$$ {\rm sin}(\theta){\partial\over \partial\theta}\left({\rm sin}(\theta){\partial\over\partial\theta}\right)\Theta(\theta) -C\,{\rm sin}^2(\theta)=D\Theta(\theta)$$
$$ {\partial^2\Phi(\phi)\over \partial\phi^2}=-D\Phi(\phi)$$

Last session we learnt that Mass and Energy are related by the famous formula: $$E_{internal}=m\,c^2\,.$$ This is a most striking relation: mass measures energy. This includes all internal energy in whatever form it might take. We will talk about more about this in the next class.

It is a direct consequence of Einsteins postulates.

Finally, defining kinetic energy as the energy associated only to the motion of the particle, i.e, kinetic-energy$\equiv E_k=$(internal Energy)-(Energy at rest), we have:


  1. ${1\over 2}m v^2$
  2. $\gamma_u m c^2$
  3. $(\gamma_u -1)m c^2$
  4. I don't know



$E_K=(\gamma_u -1)m c^2\quad$ looks very different to the non-relativistic counter-part, but is easy to see that a low speeds, this can be approximated by, $$\lim_{v<< c}E_K\sim\left(1+{1\over 2}{v^2\over c^2}-1\right)m\,c^2={1\over 2}m\, v^2\,.$$ How this Energy behaves as a function of the particle speed?. We can plot the Energy as a function of speed.

The momentum formula agrees with the non-relativistic one at low speeds as we have argued previously, but diverges as $u$ approaches $c$. In this limit, the total energy diverge. For this reason alone, it should not be possible to accelerate a massive object to the speed of light. Infinite energy would be required.

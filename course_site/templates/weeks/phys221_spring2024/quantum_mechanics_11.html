Refresher: Typical Scheme to Find the wavefunction

  1. Divide the space in suitable regions, if possible.
  2. Find the solutions to the Schrodinger equation in each of those regions.
  3. Apply the proper boundary conditions to the solutions on each region.
  4. Apply the proper boundary conditions to the derivative of the solutions on each region.
  5. Solve the conditions imposed by the boundary conditions on each region.

The Potential Step

Consider the following potential energy,



Easiest way to picture it, is to think of an incoming wave from the left towards the step, striking the step to then bounce back and/or get transmitted to the region inside the step, pretty much as light striking a glass. It is useful to remember:

Consider the wave function $\Psi(x,t)=A\,e^{i(k x-\omega t)}$. The wave is traveling...


  1. ... to the right (positive $x-$direction)
  2. ... to the left (negative $x-$direction)
  3. ...stationary
  4. I don't know


Since we are considering an stationary problem, where all wave functions solutions will have the same temporal dependence $e^{-i\omega t}$, our main concern is the spatial dependence, $$ e^{i k x}\,\quad{\rm Right\,mover}$$ $$ e^{-i k x }\,\quad{\rm Left\,mover}\,.$$ With that we can start looking into the time-independent Schrodinger equation. Let us start considering region $x<0$, the one to the left of the step, i.e $u(x)=0$. \begin{equation} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=-k^2\psi(x) \end{equation} with $k=\sqrt{2mE\over \hbar^2}$. As we have seen, solutions are $ \psi(x)=A~e^{\pm i(kx)}\,.$ Or more correctly: $$ \psi(x)=A~e^{+i(kx)}+B~e^{- i(kx)}\,.$$

For the region to the right of the step $x > 0$, or $u(x)=U_0$, we have: \begin{equation} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=-{2 m (E- U_0)\over \hbar^2}=-k'^2\psi(x) \end{equation} with $k'=\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)\over \hbar^2}$. Two possibilities arises:

$E > U_0$ and $E < U_0$.

Consider the case $E < U_0$, what of the following is correct?


  1. $k'^2>0 $
  2. $k'^2 <0 $
  3. $k'^2=0 $
  4. I don't know


Therefore, if $E > U_0$, the solutions are as before but with a different constant $k$: $$ \psi(x)=A~e^{+i k' x}+B~e^{- i k' x}\,.$$

whereas for the case $E < U_0$, the solutions are bit different: $$ \psi(x)=C~e^{+k' x}+D~e^{-k' x}\,.$$

In order to find the undetermined constant we need to apply boundary conditions. The boundary conditions at $\,x=\pm\infty\,$ for the potential step are the simplest. Is easier to think first about region $x>0$. There, the wave is transmitted, what does that tell you about $A, B, C, D$?

Think-Pair-Share


  1. $A=0, B= 0, C=0, D=0$
  2. $A=0, B\neq 0, C=0, D\neq 0$
  3. $A\neq 0, B=0, C\neq 0, D= 0$
  4. $A\neq 0, B= 0, C=0, D\neq 0$
  5. $A= 0, B\neq 0, C\neq 0, D= 0$


Whereas for region $x<0$, what can we say about the constants $A, B$?


  1. $A=0, B= 0$
  2. $A=0, B\neq 0$
  3. $A\neq 0, B=0$
  4. $A\neq 0, B\neq 0$
All in all the final spatial part of the wave function looks like:

If $E > U_0$, $$ \psi(x)_{x<0}=A~e^{+i k x}+B~e^{- i k x}\,\quad \psi(x)_{x>0}=C~e^{+i k' x} \,,$$

If $E < U_0$, $$ \psi(x)_{x<0}=A~e^{+i k x}+B~e^{- i k x}\,\quad \psi(x)_{x>0}=C~e^{- k' x} \,,$$ corresponding to: $$\psi(x<0)=\psi_{\rm Incident}+\psi_{\rm Reflected}\,,\quad\psi(x)_{x>0}= \psi_{\rm Transmitted} $$

The wave function and its derivative must be continuous at the point where the two regions meet. For $\,E>U_0\,$ these conditions are,



We can use this two equations to eliminate two constants (solve for), say $B$, and $C$: $$B={k-k'\over k+k'}A\,\quad{\rm and}\quad C={2k'\over k+k'}A\,.$$

We are concerned here with Reflection and Transmission probabilities, first notice, $${\rm Prob.\over time}={\rm Prob.\over distance}{\rm distance\over time}=|\psi(x)|^2 v=|\psi(x)|^2{\hbar k\over m}\,.$$
$$T={\rm Prob.Transmitted\over time}/{\rm Prob.Incident\over time}={|\psi_{\rm Incident}|^2 k_{x>0}\over |\psi_{\rm Transmitted}|^2 k_{x<0}}={|C|^2 k'\over |A|^2 k}$$

Equivalently, we define $$R={\rm Prob.Reflected\over time}/{\rm Prob.Incident\over time}\,,$$ which then equals


  1. ${|B|^2 k'\over |A|^2 k}$
  2. ${|B|^2 \over |A|^2 }$
  3. ${|B|^2 k\over |A|^2 k'}$
  4. Don't know


Finally, replacing $k'=\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)\over \hbar^2}$ and $k=\sqrt{2mE\over \hbar^2}$, we can write $$T=4{\sqrt{E(E-U_0)}\over\left( \sqrt{E}+\sqrt{E-U_0}\right)^2}\,,\quad{\rm and}\quad R={\left( \sqrt{E}-\sqrt{E-U_0}\right)^2\over\left( \sqrt{E}+\sqrt{E-U_0}\right)^2} $$

If $E < U_0$ all the steps proceed as in the case $E > U_0$, but now the quantity ${E-U_0}<0$ or $\sqrt{E-U_0}=i\sqrt{U_0-E}$, which lead us to: $$ R={\left( \sqrt{E}-i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)^2\over\left( \sqrt{E}+i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)^2}\,, $$ which equals,


  1. $ R=e^{\left(2 \sqrt{E}-i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)}$
  2. $R=1$
  3. $R = 0$
  4. Dont know


In turn, $$T=4{i\sqrt{E(U_0-E)}\over\left( \sqrt{E}+i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)^2}\,,$$ is complex. Is that ok? ______

Once the particle is reflected, it is completly reflected $R=1$, but if gets transmitted it actually decays exponentially $$ \psi(x)_{x>0}=C~e^{- k' x}\,.$$ We define the penetration depth to be given by $$\delta={1\over k'}={\hbar\over \sqrt{2m(U_0-E)}}\,. $$

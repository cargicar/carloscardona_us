Bound States

A bound state is one in which a particle's motion is restricted within a finite region by an external force: ex. A mass on a spring, a planet orbiting a star, and electron inside an atom, etc. Take the example of a mass on a spring. The mass is confined to move in a narrow region confined by a parabolic potential (Hooke's potential).



The total energy of this mass is constant in time and it's potential energy is time-independent. This will be a situation where at the quantum level the separation of variables is suitable. We can just plug in Hooke's potential into SE and consider a wave inside it instead of a particle, but the solution in such situation is a bit complicated. Let us do some simplifications that will allow us to solve the SE rapidly and still learn some important lessons for a quantum particle in a infinity tall potential.

Infinite Well

Lets consider the following potential energy $$u(x)=0\quad {\rm for}\quad x\quad\in\quad[0,L] $$and$$ u(x)=\infty \quad\rm{ elsewhere. }$$



Inside the well, $u(x)=0$, and then the time-independent SE simplies to: \begin{equation} -{\hbar^2\over 2m} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=E\psi(x)\quad{\rm or}\quad\, {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=-k^2\psi(x) \end{equation} with $k=\sqrt{2mE\over \hbar^2}$. What solution whould it be?


\begin{equation} \psi(x)=A~e^{i(kx)}=A~cos(kx)+iA~sin(kx) \end{equation}

Boundary conditions

What is the value of $\psi$ outside the walls?.


$\infty\psi(x<0)=E\psi(x<0)\quad\rightarrow\quad \psi(x<0)\to{1\over \infty}\to 0$.

We also need the wavefunction to be continuous, i.e $\psi(0)=0$ and $\psi(L)=0$. But why?

  1. A discontinuous functions is automatically not differentiable.
  2. A discontinuous functions does not solve not the Schrondinger equation.
  3. A discontinuous functions is not physically acceptable.
  4. Who knows!


That eliminates the first term $A~cos(kx)$. The second is: \begin{equation} A~sin(kL)=0\,. \end{equation}



Recall $k=\sqrt{2mE\over \hbar^2}$ \begin{equation} kL=n\pi\quad\rightarrow\quad E={n^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL}\,. \end{equation} Just by asking continuity of the wave function, we once more arrive to one of the most fascinating predictions of quantum mechanics, the particles energy is quantized, it can not take arbitrary continuous values, but the particle need to sit in one of this energy levels.

We still have one physical requirement: the sum of all probabilities should be one: \begin{equation} \int_{\rm all~space}|\Psi(x,t)|^2=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi(x)|^2 dx=\int_{0}^{L}|A sin(kx)|^2 dx=1\,, \end{equation} $A^2\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|sin(kx)|^2 dx=A^2{L\over 2}$, then the final solution looks like: \begin{equation} \boxed{ \psi_n(x)=\sqrt{2\over L}sin{n\pi x\over L}} \end{equation} and $ E={n^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL}$.

The solution is the tipical solution of a standing wave, and tipically we encounter that quantum mechanically, bound states are standing waves, which gives a more appealing explanation to Bohr postulate.





The nodes of the standing wave corresponds to regions of probability zero of finding the particle, yet the particle can be at either side of the node, without even crossing it!.

Also notice that a we can't have $E_k=0$ or equivalently $n=0$, since that implies a particle at rest, but that would violate the uncertanty principle, for having a position uncertanty or order $L$ can not have a zero momentum uncertanty. Therefore, the lowest energy is attained at $n=1$, it's known as the ground state energy, and the corresponding standing wave with that energy is known as ground state.

The classical limit corresponds to large $n$ or very short wave length.

Typical Scheme to Find the wavefunction

  1. Divide the space in suitable regions, if possible.
  2. Find the solutions to the Schrodinger equation in each of those regions.
  3. Apply the proper boundary conditions to the solutions on each region.
  4. Apply the proper boundary conditions to the derivative of the solutions on each region.
  5. Solve the conditions imposed by the boundary conditions on each region.

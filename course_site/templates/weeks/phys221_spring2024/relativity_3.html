Galilean Transformations

Lets us ignore Einstein's postulates for a couple of minutes, that is to say, our train is now moving very slow in comparison to the speed of light. For the sake of comparison, everything with a sub label $T$ are in-the-train-coordinates and everything with a $B$ sub label refers to ground-coordinates.

The passenger moving inside the train at constant speed $v$ throws a ball at speed $u_T$, so after a time interval $t$ the ball is at position $x_T=u_T\,t$, but according to Bob, the speed of the ball is
  1. $u_B=u_T+v$
  2. $u_B=u_T$
  3. I don't know


According to Bob, after some time $t_B$, the position of the ball is, $$x_B=u_B t_B=(u_t+v)t_B=x_T+v\, t $$ where we have used that for very low speeds $t_B=t_T=t$.

The relations above are the one-dimensional version of Galilean Transformations. They however, clearly can't satisfy Einstein's postulates, because if we change the ball by a light signal, Bob will observe $c=u_T+v > c$!

Lorentz Transformations (quick derivation)

With the goal of moving towards more general situations, lets think our Passenger-Bob system in terms of slightly more abstract entities. Consider the following two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$:

You can think $S$ as the train and $S'$ as Bob. Let us think first the Event with coordinate $x'$ as being Bob arms length and remember that $L_{P}={ L_{B}\over \gamma_v}$. Now, the Event is also moving with respect to the train, so the coordinates of the Event as measure by the passenger ($S'$) are $$x_P=v\,t+{x_B \over \gamma_v},\quad {\rm or}\quad x=v\,t+{x' \over \gamma_v}$$ or in other words, $$x'=(x-v\,t)\gamma_v =\frac{x-v\,t}{\sqrt{1-{(v/c)^2}}}$$ Now, this change of coordinates should remain valid regardless the speed of the object (as long as $v< c $, of course). Say the Event is instead a light signal (kinda Bob arms are stretching at the speed of light, if you which). The coordinates of the Event in both frames are respectively $x'=c t'$ and $x=c t$. Using the (old) trick ($0=0$) $$x^2-c^2 t^2=x'^2-c^2 t'^2\,,$$ inserting $x'=\frac{x-v\,t}{\sqrt{1-{(v/c)^2}}}$ and solving for $t'$, we get $$ t'=\frac{t-v\,x/c^2}{\sqrt{1-(v/c)^2}}\,.$$

All in all the Lorentz transformations in one dimension are:
$$ t'=\frac{t-v\,x/c^2}{\sqrt{1-(v/c)^2}}\,.$$ $$x'=\frac{x-v\,t}{\sqrt{1-{(v/c)^2}}}$$ $$y'=y$$ $$z'=z$$ or equivalently if we want to go from Bob's frame $S'$ to the passenger frame $S$, we only need to flip the sign of the relative speed: $$ t=\frac{t'+v\,x'/c^2}{\sqrt{1-(v/c)^2}}\,.$$ $$x=\frac{x'+v\,t'}{\sqrt{1-{(v/c)^2}}}$$ $$y=y'$$ $$z=z'$$ Notices that distances perpendicular to the direction of relative motion are not subject to length contraction.

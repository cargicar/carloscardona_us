Refresher: Particle-wave propagation over potential energy changes

Potential Barrier



Imagine an incoming wave from the left to the right. At $x=0$ the wave meet the barrier (or equivalently, a force $F=-du/dx$ due to the barrier potential), part of the wave is then bounced back and some part is transmitted. Region I and III: \begin{equation} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=-k^2\psi(x)\,, \quad{\rm with}\quad k=\sqrt{2mE\over\hbar^2} \end{equation} Region II: \begin{equation} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=-k'^2\psi(x)\,, \quad{\rm with}\quad k'=\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)\over\hbar^2} \end{equation} Let us start considering the region $E> U_0$.
As in before, we should have a wave component propagating to the right $e^{i(+kx-\omega t)}$ and other to the left $e^{i(-kx-\omega t)}$. In other words, we expect that the spatial wave in region $I$ to take the form: \begin{equation} \psi_I(x)= A_Ie^{+ikx} +B_Ie^{-ikx}\,, \end{equation} Whereas for region $II$


  1. $ \psi_{II}(x)=A_{II}e^{+ik'x}\,,$
  2. $ \psi_{II}(x)=B_{II}e^{-ik'x}\,,$
  3. $ \psi_{II}(x)=A_{II}e^{+ik'x} +B_{II}e^{-ik'x}\,,$
  4. Dont know


i.e $$ \psi_{II}(x)=A_{II}e^{+ik'x} +B_{II}e^{-ik'x}\,,$$ and finally for region III,


  1. $ \psi_{III}(x)= A_{III}e^{+ikx}\,, $
  2. $ \psi_{III}(x)= B_{III}e^{-ikx}\,,$
  3. $ \psi_{III}(x)= A_{III}e^{+ikx}+B_{III}e^{-ikx}\,,$
  4. Dont know


i.e, \begin{equation} \psi_{III}(x)= A_{III}e^{+ikx}\,. \end{equation}

Even before solving for boundary conditions, can you draw a sketch of the whole trip?

Now we need to impose the continuity and smoothness conditions at the boundaries: \begin{equation} \psi_{I}(0)=\psi_{II}(0) \quad\rightarrow\quad A_I+B_I=A_{II}+B_{II} \end{equation} \begin{equation} {d\psi_{I}\over dx}(0)={d\psi_{II}\over dx}(0) \quad\rightarrow\quad k(A_I-B_I)=k'(A_{II}-B_{II}) \end{equation} \begin{equation} \psi_{II}(L)=\psi_{III}(L) \quad\rightarrow\quad A_{II}e^{ik'L}+B_{II}e^{-ik'L}=A_{III}e^{ikL} \end{equation} \begin{equation} {d\psi_{II}\over dx}(L)={d\psi_{III}\over dx}(L) \quad\rightarrow\quad ik'(A_{II}e^{ik'L}-B_{II}e^{-ik'L})=ik A_{III}e^{ikL} \end{equation} Reflection probability and transmission probability are now given by: \begin{equation} R={|B_I|^2\over |A_{I}|^2} \end{equation} \begin{equation} T={|A_{III}|^2\over |A_{I}|^2} \end{equation} What happened to the $k$ dependence?

From the boundary conditions, we can solve for $B_I$ and $A_{III}$ in terms of $A_I$. The algebra is a little lengthly, but straigthforward. Doing so, and considering first the case $E>U_0$, we obtain: \begin{equation} R={sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]\over sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} \begin{equation} T={4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]\over sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} " Noticed" that $R+T=1$, as they should, since the total probability should sum up to one.

The result for the reflection probability lead us to an interesting phenomena: it can be zero sometimes!: $R=0$ when


  1. $ \sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar = n\pi/2\,, $
  2. $ \sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar = n\pi\,, $
  3. $ \sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar = 2\,n\pi\,, $
  4. Dont know


\begin{equation} R=0 \quad {\rm if} \quad sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]=0\quad\rightarrow \sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar=n\pi \end{equation} \begin{equation} \boxed{ E=U_0+{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over2mL^2} } \end{equation} This is known as resonant transmission, and implies that a particle can cross a barrier complete, unscratched. This does not imply energy quantization, since unlike bound states, unbound states energies can take any value, it just mean that only certain energies can be transmitted entirely. Also observe that in order for this effect to take place, the kinetic energy of the particle should be bigger than the barrier potential.

We can now look into the situation where a particle come towards the barrier with kinetic energy smaller than the potential barrier, i.e, $E< U_0$. We can go ahead and repeat the same analysis from top for such case, but is not really necessary: we just need to use the fact that $sin(i x)=i~ sinh^2(x)$, to get: \begin{equation} R={sinh^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]\over sinh^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} \begin{equation} T={4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]\over sinh^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} Even thought in this case we dont observe resonant transmission, we still find a non-zero probability for a particle to cross the barrier, despite not having enough energy to surmount the potential barrier. This effect is known as tunneling, and has large technological implications.



In many applications the probability of transmission is very small. This happens because in most experimental cases, the "wide" barrier condition is satisfied: $$ \sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar >>1\,, $$ this happens either when $L$ is large or $E $ is much smaller than $U_0$. \begin{equation} \boxed{ T\sim 16{E\over U_0}\left(1-{E\over U_0}\right)e^{-2L\sqrt{2m(U_0-E)}/\hbar}} \end{equation}

Tunneling has found many technological applications (as well as the explanation of several phenomena, like Radioactive decay and nuclear fusion in starts), to mention a few: Tunnel diode and Tunnel field transistors which are some new type of superconductors, tunnel junction that serve to measure very small magnetic fields such as those produced in our brains and hearts!; However the most widely and still growing technology that uses it is the The scanning tunneling microscope, which uses the strong dependence of tunneling on the barrier widht.

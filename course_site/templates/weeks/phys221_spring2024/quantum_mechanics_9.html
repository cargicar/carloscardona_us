Quick refresher: Bound States

A bound state is one in which a particle's motion is restricted within a finite region by an external force: ex. A mass on a spring, a planet orbiting a star, and electron inside an atom, etc.

Infinite Well

Lets consider the following potential energy $$u(x)=0\quad {\rm for}\quad x\quad\in\quad[0,L] $$and$$ u(x)=\infty \quad\rm{ elsewhere. }$$



solution looks like: \begin{equation} \boxed{ \psi_n(x)=\sqrt{2\over L}sin{n\pi x\over L}} \end{equation} and $ E={n^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL}$.

The solution is the tipical solution of a standing wave, and tipically we encounter that quantum mechanically, bound states are standing waves.





The nodes of the standing wave corresponds to regions of probability zero of finding the particle, yet the particle can be at either side of the node, without even crossing it!.

Also notice that a we can't have $E_k=0$ or equivalently $n=0$, since that implies a particle at rest, but that would violate the uncertanty principle, for having a position uncertanty or order $L$ can not have a zero momentum uncertanty. Therefore, the lowest energy is attained at $n=1$, it's known as the ground state energy, and the corresponding standing wave with that energy is known as ground state.

Quantum Harmonic Oscillator



"Physics is that subset of human experience which can be reduced to coupled harmonic oscillators."

-- Michael Peskin

The Harmonic Oscillator is a bounded system with a quadratic potential $$u(x)=\frac{1}{2}\kappa x^2$$
Since the potential is time-independent, we only need to look into the time-independent Schrodinger equation, \begin{equation} \boxed{ -{\hbar^2\over 2m} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}+u(x)\psi(x)=E\psi(x)}\,. \end{equation}

HO Solutions

Solutions are given by, \begin{equation} \psi_n(x)={\cal N}_n\, e^{-\frac{m\omega x^2}{2\hbar}}H_n\left(\sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}}\right),\quad n=0,1,2,\cdots. \end{equation} with, \begin{equation} H_n(z)=(-1)^n\,e^{z^2}\frac{d^n}{dz^n}\left(e^{-z^2}\right)\,,\quad{\rm and}\quad {\cal N}_n=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2^n n!}}\left(\frac{m\omega}{\pi\hbar}\right)^{1/4} \end{equation} First few Hermite polynomial: \begin{equation} H_0(z)=1,\quad H_1(z)=2z,\quad H_2(z)=4z^2-2,\quad \end{equation}


with corresponding energies given by, \begin{equation} E_n=\left(n+\frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar \omega\,.\quad n=0,1,2,\cdots. \end{equation} Notice that in this case $n$ can equal zero.

Recall that the angular frecuency of a pendulum is given by \begin{equation} \omega_{\rm classi.}=\sqrt{\frac{g}{L}}\,. \end{equation}

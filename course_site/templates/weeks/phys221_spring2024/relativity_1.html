Some (very short) Historical Remarks



Classical Physics attained a cohesive form in the late 1600 with the work of Sir Isaac Newton. It went unchallenged for two centuries, until the end of the 1800s. Additionally, Electromagnetism was a complete theory by the mid of the 19th century mainly from the work of James Clerk Maxwell. Such was the state of affairs of physics around the turn of the twentieth century (late 1800s, early 1900s) that many physics thought physics was a completely understood subject, so much so, that one of the most important contributors to the revolution about to come and one of the most renowned scientist of this very university, Albert A. Michelson, stated:

"The more important fundamental laws and facts of physical science have all been discovered, and these are now so firmly established that the possibility of their ever being supplanted in consequence of new discoveries is exceedingly remote."

However, around that same time a series of experimental results were unexplained by the current understanding of the physical laws. To mention a few:
  • Uranus and Mercury Orbits: The orbit of the planet Uranus (and later Mercurys) was found to deviate slightly from what Newtonâ€™s laws predicted
  • Black body radiation and the UV catastrophe:

    The thermal electromagnetic emission of a "purely thermal" system, was carefully measured giving, reasonably, a finite result, however Maxwell EM theory predicted an infinite result!!
  • Photoelectric Effect:

    By shining light on a conductor metal, it was observed that electrons where ejected from the metal surface, regardless the intensity of the light but strongly dependent on the color of the light. by Maxwell theory, the opposite should have occurred!.
  • Atomic spectra:

    Light emitted from atoms gives a discrete pattern of colors, not a continuous pattern as expected.
  • Michelson-Morley:
    Think of two cars (red and blue) moving in one dimension (say $x-$ direction). You are going in the red car at 10m/h and see a blue car ahead of you. You observe the blue car is neither getting farther away from you nor getting closer. What can you said about the speed of the blue car:
    1. Is less than the red one?
    2. Is the same as the red one.
    3. Is greater than the red one.
    4. I dont know.


    While still going in the red car at 10m/h you see another blue car ahead of you coming towards you. You observe this new blue car is coming towards you at 20m/h. What can you said about the speedometer of the (incoming) blue car:
    1. Is displaying 20 m/h
    2. Is displaying 10 m/h
    3. Is displaying 0 m/h
    4. I dont know.
    In 1887, Michelson (a professor of physics at the Case School of Applied Science), and Morley (a professor of chemistry at Western Reserve University), measure the difference between the speed of light as observed when coming in the direction of Earth motion and any other direction. Within experimental error, this difference was negligible.
    The speed of light is independent of earth motion!.
In the search for solving the puzzles brought by this experiments, a giant revolution took place in the early 1900s, which completely changes our understanding of the laws of nature, and as a consequence,many aspects of all of our lives. This advances can be split in three main subjects: The theory of Special Relativity, Quantum Mechanics and Quantum Statistical Mechanics, which encompass what we now call Modern Physics.

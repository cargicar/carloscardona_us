Experimental Evidence of Angular Momentum Quantization

The full quantum treatment of the hydrogen atom, led us to unavoidably consider the idea of angular momentum quatization. So now we will look into the experiment confirming this claim.
Before moving into that, we need to remember few basic facts about rotating charge particles.

Angular momentum and magnetic dipole

Lets again imagine an electron orbiting over a closed circular trajectory. Given a loop of electrical current $I$ enclosing an area $A$, the expression describing the magnetic dipole moment is given by, $$\mu= I A\,.$$ Assuming the electron is orbiting across a circular loop of radius $r$ at speed $v$. How much time does this particle takes to complete a single orbit? (Remeber, $v={\rm distance}/t_{orbit}$)

  1. $t_{orbit}={\pi r^2\over v}$
  2. $t_{orbit}={2\pi r\over v}$
  3. $t_{orbit}={ r\over v}$
  4. None of the above


Let us also recall that a current produce by a single electron orbiting a circular loop corresponds to $$I={\rm charge}/t_{orbit}={-e\over t_{orbit}}\,.$$ hence the magnetic dipole moment generated by such current, will be: $$\mu= I A={-e v\over 2\pi r}\pi r^2={-e v\over 2} r\,.$$ Finally, recalling the definition of angular momentum $\vec{L} = \vec{r}\times\vec{p}\,,$ we can relate dipole magnetic moment to angular momenta by,

  1. $\mu={-e\over 2 m_{e}}L\,.$
  2. $\mu={-e\over 2 \pi r}L\,.$
  3. $\mu={-e\over 2 p}L\,.$
  4. None of the above


It should be actually a vectorial relation, $$\vec{\mu}=-{e\over 2 m_e}\vec{ L}.$$
When a magnetic dipole moment experiences an external magnetic field, it precess due to torque $\vec{\mu}\times \vec{B}$. Such direction is therefore special.


when it does, the projection of $\mu$ over the direction of $B$, namely $\mu_z$ is constant.

We can then measure the force felt by a rotating charged particle under the influence of a magnetic field $ U=-\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B}\,,$ $$\vec{F}=-\vec{\nabla} U=\vec{\nabla}(\vec{\mu}\cdot\vec{B})\,.$$

We now know that $\vec{\mu}$ is related to the angular momenta of the orbiting charged particle. Therefore measuring the force produced by a magnetic field acting on it, will allow us to measure angular momenta, and henceforth to test the prediction on angular momentum quantization made by the Schrodinger solutions to the Hydrogen atom.

The Stern-Gerlach Experiment

Stern-Gerlach experiment.



By using the relation between magnetic dipole moment and angular momenta, we should see a quantization of force by passing atoms through a magnetic field. For the particular $B-$configuration shown above, the force is given by, $$\vec{F}=\mu_z{\partial B_z\over \partial z}\hat{z}=\left(-\frac{e}{2m_e}L_z\right)\frac{\partial B_z}{\partial z}\hat{z}\,. $$ According to the momentum quantization property found from the solutions to the wave function of the Hydrogen atom, $$L_z=m_{\ell}\hbar\,,\qquad -\ell\leq m_{\ell}\leq \ell\,.$$
Say we prepare an bunch of Hydrogen atoms at the state $n=2$, $\ell =1$, and send them (one by one) through an Stern-Gerlach setup. What we should expect to observe on the screen?

  1. A continuous vertical line.
  2. One fat dot at the middle.
  3. Two fat dots center around the middle screen.
  4. Three fat dots center around the middle screen.
  5. None of the above


The experiment confirm sucesfully the quantization of angular momenta in atoms, seeing discrete splitings along the z-axis of the screen for $\ell=1,2,\cdots$, $$\vec{F}=-{e\over 2 m_e}m_{\ell}\,\hbar\, {\partial B_z\over \partial z} \hat{z},\qquad -\ell\leq m_{\ell}\leq \ell\,, $$ in particular for $\ell =1$:

But the experiment found an incredible surprise.

If the usual atomic rules for the quantum numbers $(n,\ell,m)$ hold, how many allowable $m$ states are there for an state at $\ell=1/2$ object? (Spoiler alert: assuming it was allowed by nature)


  1. 1
  2. 2
  3. 3
  4. None of the above


Spin

It turns out that when the Stern-Gerlach experiment is perform with Hydrogen at the ground state, or $\ell=0$, we know that the only possible value of the $m_{\ell}=0$, and we should see a single small cloud (fat dot) right in the middle of the screen. But the experiment produces something like the second box below:

It was an indication of the intrinsic magnetic dipole moment of the electron! or the Spin, which satsifies the following,
  • Similarly as orbital angular momenta, spin is quantized in terms of a discrete, but not neccesarilly integer $s$ by can be semi-integer, $$ \boxed{|S|=\hbar\sqrt{s(s+1)}}.$$ Also, similarly to $L_z$, we have $$ \boxed{S_z=\hbar m_s\,\quad m_{s}= -s,-s+1,\cdots,s\,,\qquad{\rm or}\quad -s\leq m_s\leq s}\,. $$
  • Unlike $\ell$, $s$ is not a quantum number that can be changed by say, excite an state, but is number that depends on the fundamental particle we are considering. An electron for example has $s=1/2$, whereas a photon has $s=1$.
  • The intrinsic magnetic dipole $\mu_S=g{q\over 2m}S$, where $g$ is known as the gyromagnetic radius
Returning to the Stern-Gerlach experiment, what they really observed at $\ell=0$ was, $$\vec{F}=-{e\over m_e}m_{s}\,\hbar\, \partial_z B_z \hat{z},\qquad-{1\over2} \leq m_{s}\leq {1\over 2}\,,$$ since for the electron $s=1/2$, then $m_s=\pm {1\over 2}$

Spin and Wave funtions

All in all, we have just found that in our description of the wave function for an electron in an hyrogen atom, we are missing one quantum number, i.e, each state should be labeled by the quantum numbers $(n,\ell,m_{\ell},m_{s})$. Fortunately, for the case of the electron, only two values of $m_s$ are allowed, and we can just split our wave functions as, $$ \boxed{ \psi_{n,\ell,m_{\ell}}^{+}\qquad {\rm spin\,\,up}\,\qquad \psi_{n,\ell,m_{\ell}}^{-}\qquad {\rm spin\,\,down}} $$

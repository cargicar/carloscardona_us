Schrodinger equation in three dimensions

We have seen previously that in one dimension, the Schrodinger equation looks like: \begin{equation} -{\hbar^2\over 2 m}{\partial^2\over \partial x^2}\Psi(x,t)+U(x)\Psi(x,t)=i\hbar{\partial\over \partial t}\Psi(x,t)\,, \end{equation} and we also argued that this equation is based on energy considerations for a particle-wave object.

The generalization to higher dimensions is almost obvious: \begin{equation} \boxed{ -{\hbar^2\over 2 m}\left({\partial^2\over \partial x^2}+{\partial^2\over \partial y^2}+{\partial^2\over \partial z^2}\right)\Psi(x,y,z,t)+U(x,y,z,t)\Psi(x,y,z,t)=i\hbar{\partial\over \partial t}\Psi(x,y,z,t)\,, } \end{equation} However, higher dimensions introduce some complexities. For example, the SE written in rectangular coordinates as above, does not explote the spatial symmetries of particular problems, for example usual problems with spherical symmetry. Later we are going to use other more convenient coordinate systems, in which the SE adopt slightly diferent forms.

Probability density

As in one dimension, the square of the wave function represent the probability density, i.e, Probability per unit of lenght. In three dimensions we say that the square of the wave function represent the probability per unit of volume, or in math form \begin{equation} P_x([a,b])= \int_a^b|\Psi(\vec{r},t)|^2 dV\,,\quad \int_{\rm all~~space}|\Psi(\vec{r},t)|^2 dV=1\,. \end{equation}

3D Infinite Well

The analogous system to the square potential in three dimensions is the cube potential, or the quantum-dot if an sphere is used. The infinite well, is represented by a potential that constrain a particle to live in a cube with an infinite potential energy:


or in mathematical form, \begin{equation} U(x,y,z)=0\,\qquad 0 < x < L \,,\qquad 0 < y < L\,,\qquad 0 < z < L\,, \end{equation} and $U(x,y,z)=\infty$ otherwise.

As in one dimension, this type of potential have constant energy solutions, and therefore we might try to do separation of variables once again, but now in all four variables: \begin{equation} \Psi(x,y,z,t)=X(x)Y(y)Z(z)T(t)\,. \end{equation} Let us recall that in one-dimension, say $x$, the infinite well have the following solutions, \begin{equation} \boxed{ X_n(x)=\sqrt{2\over L_x}sin{n_x\pi x\over L_x}} \end{equation} with corresponding energy, $$ E={n_x^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL_x}\,.$$

Can you guess the solution $Y(y)$?

  1. $ Y_n(y)=\sqrt{2\over L_x}sin{n\pi y\over L_y}$
  2. $Y_n(y)=\sqrt{2\over L}sin{n_y\pi y\over L}$
  3. $Y_n(y)=\sqrt{2\over L_y}sin{n_y\pi y\over L_y}$
  4. I dont know


with corresponding energy, $$ E={n_y^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL_y}.$$

Putting all dimensions together, we have, \begin{equation} \boxed{\Psi(\vec{r})=\sqrt{2^3\over L_x\,L_y\,L_z}{\rm sin}{n_{x}\pi x\over L_{x}}{\rm sin}{n_{y}\pi y\over L_{y}}{\rm sin}{n_{z}\pi z\over L_{z}}} \,. \end{equation} and the total energy is giving by: \begin{equation} \boxed{ E_{n_x,n_y,n_z}= \left({n_{x}^2\over L_x^2}+{n_{z}^2\over L_z^2}+{n_{z}^2\pi^2\over L_z^2}\right){\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2 m}}\,, \end{equation} Whereas for the temporal piece: \begin{equation} {i\hbar\over T(t)}{dT(t)\over dt}=C_{t}\quad\rightarrow\quad T(t)=e^{-i{C_{t}\over\hbar}t} \end{equation} Each triple $(n_x,n_y,n_z)$ defines a three dimensional standing wave. Each new spatial dimensions leads to a corresponding quantum number contributing to the energy.

As in one dimension, none quantum number $n_{\gamma}$ is allowed to become zero, since that will vanish the wave function and will violate the uncertanty principle. Then again, the lowest energy state, or ground state, corresponds to the energy at $(n_x,n_y,n_z)=(1,1,1)$.

What are the quantum numbers corresponding to the next energy level,

  1. $(n_x,n_y,n_z)=(2,1,1)$
  2. $(n_x,n_y,n_z)=(1,2,1)$
  3. $(n_x,n_y,n_z)=(1,1,2)$
  4. All of the above


An electron is in the $(n_x, n_y, n_z)= (1, 2, 1)$ state of a cubic 3D infinite well of side length equal to $L$. Where is the probability of finding the electron the largest? Think-Pair-Shar


  1. $(x,y,z)=(L/2,L/4,L/2)$ and $(x,y,z)=(L/4,L/2,L/2)$
  2. $(x,y,z)=(L/2,L/4,L/2)$ and $(x,y,z)=(L/2,3L/4,L/2)$
  3. $(x,y,z)=(3L/4,L/2,L/2)$ and $(x,y,z)=(L/2,3L/4,L/2)$
  4. None of the above


Unlike in one dimension, the state in the next energy level above the ground state is not unique. And for that matter any other state other that the ground state is unique, because there might be multiple states with the same energy! This phenomena is know as degeneracy.

To illustrate this a bit better, lets simplify things by taking an actual cube $L_{x}=L_y=L_z=L$, then: \begin{equation} E=(n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2){\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL^2} \end{equation} each triplet that satisfies the following equation \begin{equation} (n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2)=r^2\,, \end{equation} with $r^2$ a constant, defines a family of different wave functions with the same energy.

Say $r^2=6$ and ${\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL^2}=1$, or in other words, the energy is given by $E=6$. How many different states (defined by the quantum numbers $(n_x,n_y,n_z)$) have the same energy?

  1. None
  2. $1$
  3. $2$
  4. $3$
  5. $4$




$$ \Psi_{2,1,1}=\sqrt{2\over L} {\rm sin}{2 \pi x\over L}{\rm sin} {1\pi y\over L}{\rm sin}{1\pi z\over L}\,, \qquad\Psi_{1,2,1}=\sqrt{2\over L} {\rm sin}{1\pi x\over L}{\rm sin}{2\pi y\over L}{\rm sin}{1\pi z\over L}\,,$$ $$ \Psi_{1,1,2}= \sqrt{2\over L}{\rm sin}{1 \pi x\over L}{\rm sin} {1\pi y\over L}{\rm sin}{2\pi z\over L}\,.$$ Unlike the pedestrian definition of degeneracy, in quantum mechanics it is an indication of beauty, or more precisely, of symmetry. In the example above, it happens when the cube is a perfect cube, but if we deform it, even just a little, the degeneracy is lifted. For example, for the perfect cube $permutations[(1,1,2)]$ are corresponding to the same energy: \begin{equation} E_{1,1,2}\sim {(1^2+1^2+2^2)\over L^2}={6\over L^2} \end{equation} or more explicitly $$E_{1,1,2}= E_{1,2,1}= E_{2,1,1}.$$ What if we deform a perfect cube, say we make $L_x=2L$?

  1. None
  2. $E_{1,1,2}= E_{1,2,1}= E_{2,1,1}$
  3. $E_{1,1,2}\neq E_{1,2,1}= E_{2,1,1}$
  4. $E_{1,1,2}= E_{1,2,1}\neq E_{2,1,1}$
  5. $E_{1,1,2}\neq E_{1,2,1}\neq E_{2,1,1}$


We will have $E_{1,1,2}= E_{1,2,1}= {21\over 4 L^2}\,, E_{2,1,1}={3\over L^2}$.

More generally, any deformation of the length in any direction will lift the energy degeneracy, \begin{equation} E_{1,1,2}= {1^2\over(L+\delta L_{x})^2}+{1^2\over(L+\delta L_{y})^2}+{2^2\over(L+\delta L_{z})^2} \end{equation} is different for permutations of $[(1,1,2)]$, as long as $\delta_{x}\neq\delta_{y}\neq \delta_{y}$ (BB): $$E_{1,1,2}\neq E_{1,2,1}\neq E_{2,1,1}$$

We have considered the following potential energy,





All in all the final spatial part of the wave function looks like:

If $E > U_0$, $$ \psi(x)_{x<0}=A~e^{+i k x}+B~e^{- i k x}\,\quad \psi(x)_{x>0}=C~e^{+i k' x} \,,$$

If $E < U_0$, $$ \psi(x)_{x<0}=A~e^{+i k x}+B~e^{- i k x}\,\quad \psi(x)_{x>0}=C~e^{- |k'| x} \,,$$ corresponding to: $$\psi(x<0)=\psi_{\rm Incident}+\psi_{\rm Reflected}\,,\quad\psi(x)_{x>0}= \psi_{\rm Transmitted} $$

Let us firs imposing boundary conditions for the case $E > U_0$, $$ \psi_{x<0}(x=0)= \psi_{x>0}(x=0)\,,\quad {d\psi_{x<0}(x=0)\over dx}= {d\psi_{x>0}(x=0)\over dx}$$ Which can be used to eliminate two constants (solve for), say $B$, and $C$: $$B={k-k'\over k+k'}A\,\quad{\rm and}\quad C={2k'\over k+k'}A\,.$$

We are concerned here with Reflection and Transmission probabilities
$$ T={|\psi_{\rm Incident}|^2 k_{x>0}\over |\psi_{\rm Transmitted}|^2 k_{x<0}}={|C|^2 k'\over |A|^2 k}$$ $$ R={|\psi_{\rm Incident}|^2 k_{x>0}\over |\psi_{\rm Reflected}|^2 k_{x<0}}={|B|^2 \over |A|^2 k}$$

Finally, replacing $k'=\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)\over \hbar^2}$ and $k=\sqrt{2mE\over \hbar^2}$, we can write $$T=4{\sqrt{E(E-U_0)}\over\left( \sqrt{E}+\sqrt{E-U_0}\right)^2}\,,\quad{\rm and}\quad R={\left( \sqrt{E}-\sqrt{E-U_0}\right)^2\over\left( \sqrt{E}+\sqrt{E-U_0}\right)^2} $$

If $E < U_0$ all the steps proceed as in the case $E > U_0$, but now the quantity ${E-U_0}<0$ or $\sqrt{E-U_0}=i\sqrt{U_0-E}$, which lead us to: $$ R={\left( \sqrt{E}-i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)^2\over\left( \sqrt{E}+i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)^2}\,, $$ which equals,


  1. $ R=e^{\left(2 \sqrt{E}-i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)}$
  2. $R=1$
  3. $R = 0$
  4. Dont know




In turn, $$T=4{i\sqrt{E(U_0-E)}\over\left( \sqrt{E}+i\sqrt{U_0-E}\right)^2}\,,$$ is complex. Is that ok? ______

Once the particle is reflected, it is completly reflected $R=1$, but otherwise we can not say the particle is transmitted, because it does not propagate after crossing the "wall", it stop being a wave, in the usual sense, and it actually decays exponentially $$ \psi(x)_{x>0}=C~e^{- |k'| x}\,.$$ We define the penetration depth to be given by $$\delta={1\over |k'|}={\hbar\over \sqrt{2m(U_0-E)}}\,. $$

Potential Barrier



Imagine an incoming wave from the left to the right. At $x=0$ the wave meet the barrier (or equivalently, a force $F=-du/dx$ due to the barrier potential), part of the wave is then bounced back and some part is transmitted. Region I and III: \begin{equation} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=-k^2\psi(x)\,, \quad{\rm with}\quad k=\sqrt{2mE\over\hbar^2} \end{equation} Region II: \begin{equation} {d^2\psi(x)\over dx^2}=-k'^2\psi(x)\,, \quad{\rm with}\quad k'=\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)\over\hbar^2} \end{equation} As in before, we should have a wave component propagating to the right $e^{i(+kx-\omega t)}$ and other to the left $e^{i(-kx-\omega t)}$. In other words, we expect that the spatial wave in region $I$ to take the form: \begin{equation} \psi_I(x)= A_Ie^{+ikx} +B_Ie^{-ikx}\,, \end{equation} Similarly for region $II$ \begin{equation} \psi_{II}(x)=A_{II}e^{+ik'x} +B_{II}e^{-ik'x}\,, \end{equation} and finally for region III, \begin{equation} \psi_{III}(x)= A_{III}e^{+ikx}\,. \end{equation}

Even before solving for boundary conditions, can you draw a sketch of the whole trip?

Now we need to impose the continuity and smoothness conditions at the boundaries: \begin{equation} \psi_{I}(0)=\psi_{II}(0) \quad\rightarrow\quad A_I+B_I=A_{II}+B_{II} \end{equation} \begin{equation} {d\psi_{I}\over dx}(0)={d\psi_{II}\over dx}(0) \quad\rightarrow\quad k(A_I-B_I)=k'(A_{II}-B_{II}) \end{equation} \begin{equation} \psi_{II}(L)=\psi_{III}(L) \quad\rightarrow\quad A_{II}e^{ik'L}+B_{II}e^{-ik'L}=A_{III}e^{ikL} \end{equation} \begin{equation} {d\psi_{II}\over dx}(L)={d\psi_{III}\over dx}(L) \quad\rightarrow\quad ik'(A_{II}e^{ik'L}-B_{II}e^{-ik'L})=ik A_{III}e^{ikL} \end{equation} Reflection probability and transmission probability are now given by: \begin{equation} R={|B_I|^2\over |A_{I}|^2} \end{equation} \begin{equation} T={|A_{III}|^2\over |A_{I}|^2} \end{equation} What happened to the $k$ dependence?

From the boundary conditions, we can solve for $B_I$ and $A_{III}$ in terms of $A_I$. The algebra is a little lengthly, but straigthforward. Doing so, and considering first the case $E>U_0$, we obtain: \begin{equation} R={sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]\over sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} \begin{equation} T={4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]\over sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} " Noticed" that $R+T=1$, as they should, since the total probability should sum up to one.

The result for the reflection coefficient lead us to an interesting phenomena: it can be zero sometimes!: \begin{equation}\boxed{ R=0} \quad {\rm if} \quad sin^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]=0\quad\rightarrow \sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar=n\pi \end{equation} \begin{equation} \boxed{ E=U_0+{n^2\pi^2\hbar^2\over2mL^2} } \end{equation} This is known as resonant transmission, and implies that a particle can cross a barrier complete, unscratched (It is the equivalent of classical resonance). This does not imply energy quantization, since unlike bound states, unbound states energies can take any value, it just mean that only certain energies can be transmitted entirely. Also observe that in order for this effect to take place, the kinetic energy of the particle should be bigger than the barrier potential.

We can now look into the situation where a particle come towars the barrier with kinetic energy smaller than the potential barrier, i.e, $E< U_0$. We can go ahead and repeat the same analysis from top for such case, but is not really necessary: we just need to use the fact that $sin(i x)=i~ sinh^2(x)$, to get: \begin{equation} R={sinh^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]\over sinh^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} \begin{equation} T={4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]\over sinh^2\left[\sqrt{2m(E-U_0)}L/\hbar\right]+4(E/U_0)\left[(E/U_0)-1 \right]} \end{equation} Even thougt in this case, disappointingly, we dont observer resonant transmission, we still find a non-zero probability of a particle crossing the barrier, despite not having enough energy to surmount the potential barrier. This effect is known as tunneling, and has large technological implications. Is important to observe however, that the probability of transmission can be very small, exponentially small: \begin{equation} \boxed{ T\sim 16{E\over U_0}\left(1-{E\over U_0}\right)e^{-2L\sqrt{2m(U_0-E)}/\hbar}} \end{equation}

Tunneling has found many technological applications (as well as the explanation of several phenomena, like Radioactive decay and nuclear fusion in starts), to mention a few: Tunnel diode and Tunnel field transistors which are some new type of superconductors, tunnel junction that serve to measure very small magnetic fields such as those produced in our brains and hearts! 🤯 , however the most widely and still growing technology that uses it is the The scanning tunneling microscope, which uses the strong dependence of tunneling on the barrier widht.

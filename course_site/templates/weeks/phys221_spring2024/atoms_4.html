Schrodinger Equation Solutions for the Hydrogen Atom

PHYS-221 April 1 / 2024

Learning Objectives

  1. Learn the solutions for the Schrodinger equation corresponding to the Hydrogen atom.
  2. Learn Normalization of the wave function in spherical coordinates.


Last session we learnt that the angular component of the wavefunctions for the Hydrogen atom are given by the combination $\Theta_{\ell, m_{\ell}}\Phi_{m_{\ell}}$ which are known as the spherical harmonics

Let us plot an examples, say $\Theta_{\ell=2, m_{\ell}=0}$.

But what $(\ell,m_{\ell})$ mean?

Let us start by remembering that angular momentum $L={\bf p}\times {\bf r}$ is a vector perpendicular to the orbital plane.

Do you remember the right-hand rule?.

Say a particle is moving on a circular orbit on the $x-y$ plane, what is the direction of the angular momentum vector.

  1. $x$
  2. $y$
  3. $z$
  4. None of the above



It turns out that the combined polar and azimuthal equations are the square of the angular momentum operator, and which is quantized by the integer $\ell$, whereas the integer $m_{\ell}$ corresponds to the quantization of projection of angular momenta in the $z$ direction:

$ \boxed{L_z=\hbar m_{\ell}} $ and $ \boxed{|L|=\hbar\sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)}}$.


What about the classical limit?

Angular probabilities

Even though we are considering a system with spherical symmetry, and at a given angular momentum $\ell$ the total probability would be angular independent, as it should be for a spherical symmetric system, it is important to have understand the angular dependence in order to gain a better understanding of the total wavefunction (among other things, in practical experiments, we are able to distinguish diferent angular components, by sort of breaking the spherical symmetry by applying an interaction).

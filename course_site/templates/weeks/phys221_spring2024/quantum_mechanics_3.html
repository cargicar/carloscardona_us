Photoelectric Effect

In the 1880s, Heinrich Hertz demonstrated that a light beam directed at the surface of a metal could liberate electrons. This is called the photoelectric effect —light producing a flow of electricity—and is depicted in Figure



Classical expectation (pre-quantum mechanics)

If light were strictly a wave, we should expect:
  • If light of one wavelength is able to eject electrons, then light of any wavelength should be able to do it.
  • Independent of the wavelength, the rate at which energy arrives (the intensity)—and therefore the rate at which electrons are ejected—could be made arbitrarily large simply by increasing the intensity
  • If the intensity is low, then even though electrons might still be ejected, a measurable time lag should arise. Because a wave is diffuse, considerable time might be needed for enough energy to accumulate in the electron’s vicinity
  • Finally, at any given frequency, if the intensity is increased, the departing electrons should be more energetic. A stronger electric field should produce a larger acceleration.

    We can see where the classical theory is at odds with the experimental results. In classical theory, the photoelectron absorbs electromagnetic energy in a continuous way; this means that when the incident radiation has a high intensity, the kinetic energy is expected to be high. But the experiment shows that the maximum kinetic energy of photoelectrons is independent of the light intensity.

    Cut -off frequency

    It was additionally observed, that low-intensity light of $500 nm$ wavelength ejects electrons from sodium, with no time lag, while light of $600 nm$ wavelength cannot, even at many times the intensity, but according to the classical description, the kinetic energy of photoelectrons should not depend on the frequency of incident radiation at all, and there should be no cut-off frequency.

    What the photoelectric effect is telling us


    • Stopping voltage does not depend on the intensity of light → kinetic energy is independent of light intensity.
    • For large positive electric potential between the plates and a given light intensity (i.e force pushing electron from the left plate towards the right plate), the current reach a maximum constant value → number of photoelectrons reach a maximum constant number.
    • There exist a cut-off frequency below which none photoelectrons are observed.
    This results leads to Einstein in 1905 to consider seriously Planck's hypothesis, that the energy of light come in integer packets of the frequency $E=n\,h\,f$ (depends on it's frequency in a quantized fashion). In Einstein's approach, a beam of monochromatic light of frequency $f$ is made of particles, called photons. A photon is a particle of light and carries an energy $$E_f=h\, f\,.$$ In Einstein's interpretation, each photon gives away all its energy to only one electron on the metal surface, because only integer numbers of $h\,f$ are available, so the photon can't give away just a fraction. This explains the frequency cut-off, if an photon has energy $E_f=h\,f<\phi $, then there is simply insufficient energy in any given photon to free an electron. So none are freed, no matter how high the intensity. However, if the frequency is high enough, such that $h\,f>\phi$, then electrons can be ejected,

    then the cut-off frequency will be given by the condition

    1. $f_c={\phi\over h}$
    2. $f_c={\phi}$
    3. $f_c={ h}$
    4. I don't know


    For higher frequencies, energy conservation tell us: $$E_f=E_{K_{max}}+\phi\,,\quad{\rm or}\quad\boxed{ E_{K_{max}}=h\,f-\phi}$$ The subscript $max$ refers to the fact that the maximum energy an electron can gain, is all what is left from overcoming $\phi$.

    Einstein’s interpretation of the photoelectric effect explains not only the observation that a certain minimum frequency is required but also the other classically unexpected results. If a single photon—a particle of concentrated energy rather than a diffuse wave—does have enough energy, ejection should be immediate, with no time lag. Also, the electron’s kinetic energy should depend only on the energy of the single photon—the frequency—not on how many strike the metal per unit time (the intensity). In all respects, Einstein’s explanation agrees with the experimental evidence, and the achievement earned him the 1921 Nobel Prize in physics.

    The Compton Effect

    Around 1920's, Planck's energy quantization hypothesis was getting traction. Right after Einstein used it to explain the Photo-electric effect, Arthur Holly Compton used it to explain the scattering of light off free electrons.

    According to the theory of electromagnetism of Maxwell, the electron getting hit by the incoming X-ray, will oscillate and radiate a wave of approximately the same frequency and in all directions.

    It was observed that the scattered wave has a significantly longer wavelength and directed in a particular direction.

    Let us recall that if the two relativistic formulas $E=\gamma_u\,m\,c^2$ and $p=\gamma_u\,m\,u$ are combined, we get a more universal relation between momentum an energy that can be applied equally to massive and massless particles, $$E^2=p^2c^2+m^2c^4\,.$$ for a massless particle, we then have $E=p\,c$

    Compton combine this relativistic expression with Planck's hypothesis to define the momentum of a photon to be $$\boxed{p={E\over c}={h\, f\over c}={h\over \lambda}}\,.$$ To experimentally check the validity of such assumption experimentally, Compton observed the interaction between X-rays and free electrons, and consider it as a collision between two particles, a photon and electron.

    Similarly, momentum conservation in the $y-$direction looks like: $$0={h\over \lambda'}\sin\theta-\gamma_u m_e u \sin\phi$$ and energy conservation implies $${h\,c\over \lambda}+m_e\,c^2={h\,c\over \lambda'}+\gamma_u m_e\,c^2\,.$$ We then use these equations to eliminate variable $u$, which after some algebra lead us to a relation between the wavelenghts of the light before and after the collision, $$\boxed{\lambda'-\lambda={h\over m_e c}(1-cos\theta)}\,.$$ Which is known as the Compton effect.

    The difference in wavelength between the incident and scattered photons depends only on the angle of scatter, a discovery that earned Compton the 1927 Nobel Prize. We see also that the scattered photon is always of longer wavelength than the incident. Its energy is always less, which makes sense, for kinetic energy is given to the electron.

    Pair Production and Antimatter

    The blackbody radiation, photoelectric effect and the Compton effect are three important ways in which electromagnetic radiation interacts as a particle with matter. We now discuss a third.

    In 1932, a revolutionary new particle was discovered. Carl D. Anderson (Nobel Prize, 1936) was studying the effects of cosmic rays, energetic particles bombarding Earth, when he noticed something behaving like an electron but of positive charge. It curved the right amount but the “wrong” way in a magnetic field.



    This positively charged electron was termed the positron. The phenomenon of particle creation by pairs, is known as pair production.

    The positron is an example of antimatter. Its life is short as it quickly finds an electron to annihilate together: erased, their entire energy suddenly transformed to two photons. This process is known as pair annihilation.

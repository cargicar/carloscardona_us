Lets recap last concepts

Consider the following two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$:

If the observer in frame $S'$ measures the location of the Event to be $x'$, what is the transformation to the location $x$ seen from $S$?

  1. $x=\frac{x'-v\,t'}{\sqrt{1-{(v/c)^2}}}$
  2. $x=\frac{x'+v\,t'}{\sqrt{1+{(v/c)^2}}}$
  3. $ t=\frac{t'-v\,x'/c^2}{\sqrt{1-(v/c)^2}}$
  4. $ t=\frac{t'+v\,x'/c^2}{\sqrt{1-(v/c)^2}}$


Mnemonic: $\gamma_v=\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-{(v/c)^2}}}$ approach 1 for relative slow speeds.

Equivalent we have the transformations from $S$ to $S'$: $$ t'=\frac{t-v\,x/c^2}{\sqrt{1-(v/c)^2}}=\gamma_v\,(t-v\,x/c^2).$$ $$x'=\frac{x-v\,t}{\sqrt{1-{(v/c)^2}}}=\gamma_v\,(x-v\,t).$$ $$y'=y$$ $$z'=z$$

Simultaneity

By definition, $t_1=t_2$ or $\quad t_2-t_1=0$ (in one of the frames, here lets choose $S$). What is the time difference in frame $S'$? $$t_2'-t_1'=\gamma_v\left((t_2-t_1-{v\over c^2}(x_2-x_1))\right) $$ but $\quad t_2-t_1=0$, then: $$t_2'-t_1'=\gamma_v\left((-{v\over c^2}(x_2-x_1))\right) $$ or in more compact form $$\Delta t'=\gamma_v\left((-{v\over c^2}(\Delta x))\right) $$

Time Dilation


Say passenger (Alice) is in frame $S'$, and look at her watch before passing in front of Bob (Event 1). Then she look her watch again some time after passing in front of Bob (event 2). Then we know that $x'_1=x'_2$, let's see how much time passed between the two events as seeing from Bobs frame ($S$). $$ t_2-t_1=\gamma_v\left( t'_2-t'_1+{v\over c^2}(x'_2-x'_1)\right)=\gamma_v \Delta t'. $$ We see time dilated in Bobs frame relative to the Alice frame. But wait, this is essentially the same equation we used in simultaneity... what changes?.

$$ t_2-t_1=\gamma_v \Delta t'\equiv =\gamma_v \Delta t_0 $$ where $\Delta t_0$ is known as proper time, which refers to the time that elapses between events in the frame in which events all occur at the same location. In the case we have considered here, that frame is $S'$.

Length Contraction


Consider Alice is holding a stick of length $L_0$ in her frame $S'$. In order to measure the length of the stick in her frame, we define Event 1 and Event two as being the ends of the stick, i.e, $x'_2-x'_1=L_0$. To see what is the length of the stick as seen by Bob, we apply the Lorentz transformations $x'=\gamma_v\,(x-v\,t)$: $$x'_2-x'_1=\gamma_v(x_2-x_1-v(t_2-t_1)).$$ There is an important observation here: In order for a length measure to make sense, it need to be taken at a specific time. Think Bob has another stick (ruler) of exactly the same size as the observed length of the stick in his frame. Bob can only say they are the same size in his frame, right at the time when this two sticks match. This implies, $$x'_2-x'_1=\gamma_v(x_2-x_1)$$ or $$L_B={L_0\over\gamma_v}.$$ Therefore, Bob perceived a smaller stick, or a contracted stick.

More generally, The length of an object in the frame in which it is at rest —its rest frame— is given the symbol $L_0$ and is known as proper length.

Light-year

Now that we know that the speed of light (in vacuum) is the same regardless of who is observing it, added to the fact that the speed of light is very large, is an ideal candidate to define a length distance, in particular regarding far away objects or very large objects.

How much is the speed of light?. In SI units $$c= 299792458 m/s .$$ However, for all intents and purposes in this course we will accept the very good approximation $$c=3\times 10^{8} m/s .$$ So what is a light-year?. A light-year is the distance light travels in one year. Mathematically established is just $$ly=1\,year\times c$$ (with $c$ in units of $m/year$). One light-year is about 6 trillion miles (9 trillion km), or more precisely $1 ly=9.5\times 10^{15}m.$. Since light is finite, it might take years for the light produced by very distant objects to reach us, so every time we see something very far we are actually looking at its past.

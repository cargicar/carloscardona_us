X-ray Atom-Fingerprinting

We have seen during our discussion of the Hydrogen atom, the emission and absorption of certain frecuencies of light only, characterized all different elements. In ground-state atoms, (non-excited levels), all lowest energy states are filled and the only transitions occurring are due to valence electrons moving within the highest energy levels. However, something else can happen. An acelerated free electron can hit and knock out an electron on deep inner shell, creating a hole that is quickly fill by a weak tight electron in the upper levels. This kind of transitions produce X-rays that are characteristic of the particular element, and are also used as fingerprints for elements. That's one of the rearons we want to look at the space in the X-ray spectrum. Let's estimate the wavelength of one such process.

We want to estimate the wavelength of a photon emmited by an electron transitioning from $n=2$ to $n=1$ in a neodymium (Z=20 atom). Let's approximate an atom with atomic number $Z$, by an hidrogen atom with a nucleus of size $Z$ (known as an Hydrogen-like atom). What are the corresponding energy levels?.
Lets recall the energy levels for a Hydrogen atom $$E_n=-{m(e^2)^2\over2 (4\pi\epsilon_0)^2\hbar}{1\over n^2}={-13.6\, eV\over n^2}. $$ Therefore, if the nucleus now has $q=Z\,e$, we get, $$E_n=-{m(Ze\times e)^2\over2 (4\pi\epsilon_0)^2\hbar}{1\over n^2}={-13.6\, eV}{Z^2\over n^2} $$ Aditionally, we want to account for the other electrons by estimating that before the transition the effective charge the electron feels is $Z=55$ due to screening from its lower orbital fellows, whereas it feels $Z=59$ after transitioning to the lower level.
The energy emmited, is equal to the energy release by the electron falling from $n=2$ to $n=1$: $$h\,f={hc\over \lambda}=-13.6\,eV\left( {55^2\over 2^2}-{59^2\over 1^2}\right) =3.71\times 10^4\, eV$$ or, $\lambda=30 $ pm. (X-rays go from $10$ pm to $10$ nm.) The experimental value is around the same.

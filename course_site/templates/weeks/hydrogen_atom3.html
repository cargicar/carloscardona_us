Schrodinger Equation(s) for Hydrogen atom

Previously wrote the Schrodinger equation for a system with a central potential in Spherical coordinates, and use separation of variables to get a system of three independent differential equations, one per spherical coordinate:

✓ The polar equation: $$ {\rm sin}(\theta){\partial\over \partial\theta}\left({\rm sin}(\theta){\partial\over\partial\theta}\right)\Theta(\theta) -C\,{\rm sin}^2(\theta)=D\Theta(\theta)$$
✓ The azimuthal equation: $$ {\partial^2\Phi(\phi)\over \partial\phi^2}=-D\Phi(\phi)$$
The radial equation: $$-{\partial\over \partial r}\left(r^2{\partial\over \partial r}\right)R(r)-{2mr^2\over \hbar^2}(E-U(r))R(r)=C\,R(r)$$
With potential energy given by, \begin{equation} U(r)=-{1\over4\pi \epsilon_0}{e^2\over r}\, \end{equation}

The Radial equation

Now we consider the actual equation that tell apart the Hydrogen atom from any other atom, or more generally, central potential. $$-{\hbar^2\over 2 m}{1\over r^2}{\partial\over \partial r}\left(r^2{\partial\over \partial r}\right)R(r)+{ \hbar^2 \ell(\ell+1)\over 2mr^2}R(r)+U(r)R(r)=E\,R(r)$$ $$ E_{\rm kinetic}\qquad+\qquad \left(E_{\rm rotational}={L^2\over 2m}\right)+\quad E_{\rm potential}=E_{\rm total} $$ We know that $E$ is a conserved quantity in an isolated system, and similarly as in the case of polar and azimuthal equations, the value of the constants in the Schrodinger equations can NOT be arbitrary, since not all values give us physically acceptable solutions, in the case of $E$, it needs to be, \begin{equation} E=-{m e^2\over2 (4\pi\epsilon_0)^2 n^2}={-13.6 eV\over n^2} \end{equation} which equals the energy from Bohr's model, with an important modification: $$ \boxed{|L|=\hbar\sqrt{\ell(\ell+1)},\quad \ell=0,1,2,\cdots,n-1}\,. $$ The explanation for the last restriction comes from the fact that rotational energy can't be larger than the total energy. The quantum number $n=$ for the Hydrogen atom is known as the principal quantum number.

By doing some algebra we can transform the radial equation into, $$x Z''(x) +(2(\ell+1) -x)Z'(x)+(\lambda-(\ell+1))Z(x)=0 $$ where $x=\sqrt{8 m E\over \hbar^2}r,\quad\lambda={ e^2\over \hbar}\sqrt{m\over 2 E}$ and derivatives are taken with respect to $x$ (please don't trust a lot this change of variables, I need to recheck it)
This equation is known as the associated Laguerre equation, which defined the associated Laguerre polynomials.



Degeneracy

I have claim previously that degeneracy is a reflection of symmetry. The hidrogen atom being spherically symmetric, should exhibit some degeneracy. We can quickly see that for each value of $n$, there corresponds many $\ell$'s and $m_{\ell}$'s, and since each triplet $(n,\ell,m_{\ell})$ defines a unique wavefunction $\Psi_{(n,\ell,m_{\ell})}=R_{n,\ell}\Theta_{\ell, m_{\ell}}\Phi_{m_{\ell}}$, all energy levels (except the ground state), are degenerate

Normalization

$$|\Psi_{(n,\ell,m_{\ell})}|^2=|R_{n,\ell}|^2|\Theta_{\ell, m_{\ell}}|^2 $$ which is usually split into angular and radial normalizations: $$\int_{0}^{\infty}R^2(r)r^2 dr=1,\qquad 2\pi\int_0^{2\pi}\Theta^2(\theta) {\rm sin\theta}d\theta=1 $$

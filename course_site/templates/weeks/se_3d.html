Schrodinger equation in three dimensions

We have seen previously that in one dimension, the Schrodinger equation looks like: \begin{equation} -{\hbar^2\over 2 m}{\partial^2\over \partial x^2}\Psi(x,t)+U(x)\Psi(x,t)=i\hbar{\partial\over \partial t}\Psi(x,t)\,, \end{equation} and we also argued that this equation is based on energy considerations for a particle-wave object.

The generalization to higher dimensions is almost obvious: \begin{equation} \boxed{ -{\hbar^2\over 2 m}\left({\partial^2\over \partial x^2}+{\partial^2\over \partial y^2}+{\partial^2\over \partial z^2}\right)\Psi(x,y,z,t)+U(x,y,z,t)\Psi(x,y,z,t)=i\hbar{\partial\over \partial t}\Psi(x,y,z,t)\,, } \end{equation} However, higher dimensions introduce some complexities. For example, the SE written in rectangular coordinates as above, does not explote the spatial symmetries of particular problems, for example usual problems with spherical symmetry. Later we are going to use other more convenient coordinate systems, in which the SE adopt slightly diferent forms.

Probability density

As in one dimension, the square of the wave function represent the probability density, i.e, Probability per unit of lenght. In three dimensions we say that the square of the wave function represent the probability per unit of volume, or in math form \begin{equation} \int_{\rm all~~space}|\Psi(\vec{r},t)|^2 dV=1\,, \end{equation}

Bound states

The analogous system to the square potential in three dimensions is the cube potential. The infinite well, is represented by a potential that constrain a particle to live in a cube with an infinite potential energy:


or in mathematical form, \begin{equation} U(x,y,z)=0\,\qquad 0 < x < L \,,\qquad 0 < y < L\,,\qquad 0 < z < L\,, \end{equation} and $U(x,y,z)=\infty$ otherwise.

As in one dimension, this type of potential have constant energy solutions, and therefore we might try to do separation of variables once again, but now in all four variables: \begin{equation} \Psi(x,y,z,t)=X(x)Y(y)Z(z)T(t)\,, \end{equation} Replacing that on the 3D SE, we get: \begin{equation} \sum_{\Gamma=X(x),Y(y),Z(z)} {1\over \Gamma(\gamma)}{\partial^2\Gamma(\gamma)\over\partial \gamma^2}=-{2m E\over \hbar^2}\,, \end{equation} implying that each term should be a constant: \begin{equation} {1\over \Gamma(\gamma)}{\partial^2\Gamma(\gamma)\over\partial \gamma^2}=C_{\gamma}\,, \end{equation} with $\Gamma=X,Y,Z$ and $\gamma=x,y,z$, whereas for the temporal piece: \begin{equation} {i\hbar\over T(t)}{dT(t)\over dt}=C_{t}\quad\rightarrow\quad T(t)=e^{-i{C_{t}\over\hbar}t} \end{equation} We are now in the familiar situation we were in one dimension, just that for two additional coordinates. There we have seen that the solution was of the form: \begin{equation} \Gamma(\gamma)=A_{\gamma}{\rm sin}{n_{\gamma}\pi\gamma\over L_{\gamma}}\,. \end{equation} Here is worth doing some clarifications that I have ommited during the one dimension discussion. The most general solution to equation above is \begin{equation} \Gamma(\gamma)=A_{\gamma}e^{ik\gamma}+B_{\gamma}e^{-ik\gamma}\,. \end{equation} Since the wave can't penetrate an infinite potential (it would requiere an infinite initial energy), the probability of bounce in the boundary has to be equal to the probability of the incoming wave (in other words, transmision is zero), or $|A_{\gamma}|=|B_{\gamma}|$. Additionally, we are positioning the cube as shown at the figure above, which in particular imply that we want $\Gamma(0)=0$, which impose $A_{\gamma}=-B_{\gamma}$, leading us to the result quoted. Expanding back from $\Gamma(\gamma)$ to $X,Y,Z$ we have the total solution to be: \begin{equation} \Psi(\vec{r})=2^3 A_{x}A_{y}A_{z}{\rm sin}{n_{x}\pi x\over L_{x}}{\rm sin}{n_{y}\pi y\over L_{y}}{\rm sin}{n_{z}\pi z\over L_{z}} \,. \end{equation}

The only actual difference with the case in one dimension, is that now we have three indepent solutions, which give us three contributing quantizations to the energy: \begin{equation} -{n_{x}^2\pi^2\over L_x^2}-{n_{z}^2\pi^2\over L_z^2}-{n_{z}^2\pi^2\over L_z^2}=-{2m E\over \hbar^2} \end{equation} or in slightly better form: \begin{equation} \boxed{ E_{n_x,n_y,n_z}= \left({n_{x}^2\over L_x^2}+{n_{z}^2\over L_z^2}+{n_{z}^2\pi^2\over L_z^2}\right){\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2 m}}\,, \end{equation} with corresponding wave function: \begin{equation} \boxed{ \Psi(\vec{r})= A\,{\rm sin}{n_{x}\pi x\over L_{x}}{\rm sin}{n_{y}\pi y\over L_{y}}{\rm sin}{n_{z}\pi z\over L_{z}}} \,. \end{equation} We can define a single constant $A=2^3A_{x}A_{y}A_{z}$. Each triple $(n_x,n_y,n_z)$ defines a three dimensional standing wave. Each new spatial dimensions leads to a corresponding quantum number contributing to the energy.

As in one dimension, none quantum number $n_{\gamma}$ is allowed to become zero, since that will vanish the wave function and will violate the uncertanty principle. Then again, the lowes energy state, or ground state, corresponds to the energy at $(n_x,n_y,n_z)=(1,1,1)$.

Unlike in one dimension, now is not quite obvious what state is the next energy level above the ground state (do some analysis here).

Even more, there might be multiple states with the same energy! This phenomena s know as degeneracy. To see why that is possible, lets take the actual cube $L_{\gamma}=L$, then: \begin{equation} E=(n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2){\pi^2\hbar^2\over 2mL^2} \end{equation} each triplet that satisfies the following equation \begin{equation} (n_x^2+n_y^2+n_z^2)=\epsilon\,, \end{equation} with $\epsilon$ a constant, defines a family of different wave functions with the same energy.


$$ \Psi_{2,1,1}= A{\rm sin}{2 \pi x\over L_{x}}{\rm sin} {1\pi y\over L_{y}}{\rm sin}{1\pi z\over L_{z}}\,, \qquad\Psi_{1,2,1} A{\rm sin}{1\pi x\over L_{x}}{\rm sin}{2\pi y\over L_{y}}{\rm sin}{1\pi z\over L_{z}}\,,$$ $$ \Psi_{1,1,2}= A{\rm sin}{1 \pi x\over L_{x}}{\rm sin} {1\pi y\over L_{y}}{\rm sin}{2\pi z\over L_{z}}\,.$$ Unlike the pedestrian picture of degeneracy, in quantum mechanics it is an indication of beauty (ok, personal opinion), or more precisly, of symmetry. In the example above, it happens when the cube is a perfect cube, but if we deform it, even just a little, the degeneracy is lifted. For example, for the perfect cube $n_{\gamma}=$permutations$[(1,1,2)]$ are corresponding to the same energy: \begin{equation} E_{1,1,2}\sim {(1^2+1^2+2^2)\over L^2} \end{equation} or more explicitly $$E_{1,1,2}= E_{1,2,1}= E_{2,1,1}$$ whereas the deformed cube will produce different values for each term, i.e: \begin{equation} E_{1,1,2}= {1^2\over(L+\delta L_{x})^2}+{1^2\over(L+\delta L_{y})^2}+{2^2\over(L+\delta L_{z})^2} \end{equation} is different for permutations of $[(1,1,2)]$, as long as $\delta_{x}\neq\delta_{y}\neq \delta_{y}$ (BB): $$E_{1,1,2}\neq E_{1,2,1}\neq E_{2,1,1}$$

From the two-well example we can see that some wave functions inside each energy band are odd and some others are even, depending if they component wave functions are summed in phase or half-phase. Even wave functions are bonding and odd are unbonding, as can be infered from the two well example.

n=1 bounding and anti-bonding

Say we have two Hydrogen atoms both at energy $n=1$ and state $1s$



The even combination is denote by the symbol $\sigma1s$ and is known as the sigma bonding orbital which is typically of low energy because the electron can be close to both protons. The odd one is given by the same symbol $\sigma^*1s$ and is known as the sigma antibonding orbital, typically of higher energy because the electron wnats to be closer to one or the other proton. $\sigma1s$ states want to bond atoms together, whereas $\sigma^*1s$ want to "unbond" atoms. However, it is a steel possible to have an antibonding state occupied in a bond molecule.

When molecules form? The short answer would be: as long as we have an energetically favorable configuration, or in other words, when the average energy of a configuration is lower than the single atoms energy:

Simple Multi-particle System

Think about a classical system with billions of particles interacting. We know very well the laws and principles describing the behaviour of such system: we only need to solve the Newton's equation for EACH particle. However, it is almost obvious that approach is close to imposible, when the number of particles is about $ 6.022140857\times 10^{23}=$Avogadro number$\equiv AN$. Just to have a rough idea of how large this number is, Avogadro's number is the approximate number of milliliters of water in the Pacific Ocean.

In such situation we need to developed a more useful formalism, one in which we describe the macroscopic physics, regardles of the microscopic individual state of each particle.

Here is a question we can answer using only our intuition.
Suppose we have a gas in a box with $ 6.022140857\times 10^{23}$ particles, and draw an imaginary wall splitting the box in two halves. Approximately, what proportion of particles will be at the left hand half of the chamber at a given time? ____.

Certainly, provided we have the proper technology, we can answer this question by solving the Newtonian mechanical problem, but there is a simpler approach which will also answer the question within a very good approximation. Before moving into such approach, we need to remember a basic concept

Suppose that now the gas is composed by only four distinguishable particles (say each of has a different color) and again draw an imaginary wall splitting the box in two halves.

The number of ways, $W$, of having four particles in the left half is equal to ____.

The number of ways ($W$) of having three particles in the left half is equal to ___.

The number of ways ($W$) of having two particles in the left half is equal to ____.

The number of ways ($W$) of having one particle in the left half is equal to ____.

The counting above can be easily generalized mathematically to any number of particles by using some math definitions.
  • $N!=$ Number of different ways of arranging $N$ distinguishable objects.(Think of say 3 colors, there is 6 ways of arranging them in a row.)
  • (Simpler case: two categories), i.e, $n$ objects in one category and the remaining $N-n$ in the other, $$W(n)=\begin{pmatrix}N \\ n\end{pmatrix}= {N!\over n! (N-n)!}$$ has a special name, the binomial coefficient .

Now lets go back to the gas in a box and start simple: If we have $N=4$ particles, how many arrangements have 4 particles to the left half? ${4!\over 0! 4!}=1$ what about 3 to the left? ${4!\over 1! 3!}=4$ and 2? ${4!\over 2! 2!}=6$.

An important fact from stastistics, is that, as long as all the arrangements are independent, each different way of arranging is equally probable. Henceforth, the more arrangments a given configurations has, the higher the probability. What is the total number of arragements? We have $2^N$, and so $$P(n_L)={W(n_L)\over 2^N }$$ Let's see the distributions of probabilities for different $N$ with a simple script. Notebook

We can see that the simpler statistical analysis tell us that the highest probability occurs when half particles are to the left and half to the right of the chamber, which agrees with our intuition.

Macro- and Micro-states

Say again we have $N=4$ particles in the chamber, how many arrangments give a configuration with half of the particles to the left? ____.



Each of the configurations _6_ configurations is called a ______.

All _6_ configurations belong to the same ______, i.e, to the a configuration with 2 particles to the left.

Each individual arrangement, corresponds to a different microscopic configuration, which we call a microstate= state of the system in which we have complete knowledge of the physical state of each independent particle. Known such microstate is not only realistically impossible, but unnecessary, since even though we were able to know all those $2^{10^{23}}$, they all correspond to the same macrostate, with the same macroscopic overall description with the same values of temperature, pressure, volumen, etc. In systems with billions and up particles, we are interested in the macroscopic description, and that is the goal of statistical mechanics.

Entropy and Temperature

Consider the The Second Law of Thermodynamics establishing that in an isolated system, the entropy will not decrease.
Based on our discussion(s) above (A system evolves towards equilibrium=greatest probability=greatest number microscopic configurations) .

  2. $\Delta S=S_f-S_i=\frac{1}{W_f}-\frac{1}{W_i}$
  3. $\Delta S=S_f-S_i=\log W_f-\log W_i$
  4. $\Delta S=S_f-S_i=\sqrt{W_f}-\sqrt{W_i}$



Entropy is a measure of disorder but in more accurate mathematical terms, it measures the number of ways a given macroscopic state can be achieve, or equivalently to the probability of a given macrostate. It is defined as,$$\boxed{S=k_B\log W\,,} $$ where $k_B=1.380649\times10^{-23} Jâ‹…K^{-1}$ is the Boltzman constant and $W$ is the number of microscopic configurations giving the same macroscopic state. Since the equilibrium state is the one with the greatest probability, it also is the state with the greatest entropy.

The definition in terms of a $\log$ is a matter of convenience:
  1. usually the amount of microscopic ways of attain an equilibrium state is of several orders of magnitude, and the $log$ of a large number is more manageable.
  2. The number of ways is a multiplicative quantity, whereas we want the entropy to be an additive quantity.
  3. We want to define the a state with zero entropy, as such that can only be obtained by a singe microscopic configuration $W=1$ (In te case of the gas in the box, this will happend at $n_R=0$.)


Let's once again use our intuition to define a useful quantity. Imagine again a gas in a box splited in half, this time by a membrane that allows to transfer energy from one side to the other by collisions of particles on the membrane. If one portion of the gas is heated, we expect the temperature of such side (as measure by a thermometer) to rise: Heat is defined as the transfer of energy across the boundary of a system due to a temperature difference between the system and its surroundings. From the microscopic point of view, this increase in temperature is due to the increase of the overall kinetic energy of the constituent particles. If left alone, particles hitting the membrane will transfer part of its kinetic energy to the some particles at the other side, until we reached again the equilibrum state at both sides. By the Second Law, this new equilibium state must have a higher entropy that the previous equilibrium state, and since the new state is an equilibrium state, the temperature of both sides must be the same now and the entropy should have increased.

With this intuitive picture in mind, we can associate the heat to the entropy change,or equivalently, the energy transfer from one side of the box to the other, can be measured by the change of entropy from the previous state to the new equilibrium state at temperature $T$.

When the energy being exchanged is thermal, we called Heat= $Q$. Recalling that the units of $S$ are the units of $k_{B}$, is only natural to define: $$\frac{\Delta S}{\Delta Q}=\frac{1}{T}. $$ With this definition, we can compute changes in entropy by knowing the amount of energy transfered in form of heat, $$\Delta S=\int_i^f{dQ\over T}. $$
